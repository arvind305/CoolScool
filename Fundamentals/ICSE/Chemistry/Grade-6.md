# ICSE Grade 6 Chemistry Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Chemistry syllabus for Grade 6 introduces students to the fundamental concepts of chemistry, including the nature of matter, its states, classification into pure substances and mixtures, and basic separation techniques. The curriculum builds foundational understanding through observation, experimentation, and connection to everyday life experiences.

---

## Theme 1: Matter and Its Composition
### Content

**What is Matter?**
- Definition: anything that has mass and occupies space
- Matter can be felt by one or more of our senses
- Examples of matter around us

**Classification of Matter**
- Living and non-living matter
- Natural and man-made (artificial) matter
- Solid, liquid, and gaseous matter

**Particles of Matter**
- Matter is made up of tiny particles
- Particles are too small to be seen with naked eye
- Particles are called atoms and molecules

**Properties of Particles of Matter**
- Particles have spaces between them
- Particles are always in motion
- Particles attract each other
- Diffusion: movement of particles from high to low concentration

---

## Theme 2: States of Matter
### Content

**The Three States of Matter**
- Solid, liquid, and gas
- Arrangement of particles in each state
- Properties of each state

**Solid State**
- Definite shape and definite volume
- Particles tightly packed
- Particles vibrate in fixed positions
- Strong forces of attraction between particles
- Examples: ice, wood, iron, book

**Liquid State**
- Definite volume but no definite shape
- Takes the shape of container
- Particles less tightly packed than solids
- Particles can slide past each other
- Can flow from higher to lower level
- Examples: water, milk, oil, juice

**Gaseous State**
- No definite shape and no definite volume
- Fills the entire container
- Particles far apart with large spaces between them
- Particles move freely and randomly
- Very weak forces of attraction
- Can be compressed easily
- Examples: air, oxygen, carbon dioxide

**Interconversion of States of Matter**
- Melting (Fusion): solid to liquid
- Freezing (Solidification): liquid to solid
- Evaporation: liquid to gas (at any temperature)
- Boiling: liquid to gas (at boiling point)
- Condensation: gas to liquid
- Sublimation: solid directly to gas
- Examples: ice melting, water boiling, camphor sublimating

**Effect of Heat on Matter**
- Heating increases particle motion
- Heating can change state of matter
- Cooling decreases particle motion
- Cooling can change state of matter

## Theme 3: Pure Substances and Mixtures
### Content

**Pure Substances**
- Fixed composition throughout
- Definite physical and chemical properties
- Cannot be separated by physical methods
- Types: elements and compounds
- Examples: distilled water, pure gold, common salt

**Mixtures**
- Combination of two or more substances
- Variable composition
- Components retain their individual properties
- Can be separated by physical methods
- Examples: air, salt water, sand and iron filings

**Homogeneous Mixtures (Solutions)**
- Uniform composition throughout
- Components cannot be seen separately
- Also called solutions
- Solute: substance that dissolves
- Solvent: substance in which solute dissolves
- Examples: salt in water, sugar in water, air

**Heterogeneous Mixtures**
- Non-uniform composition
- Different components can be seen
- Different parts have different composition
- Examples: sand and water, oil and water, salad

**Differences Between Pure Substances and Mixtures**
- Composition: fixed vs variable
- Properties: definite vs combination of components
- Separation: chemical vs physical methods
- Boiling/melting point: fixed vs variable

## Theme 4: Separation Techniques
### Content

**Need for Separation**
- To obtain useful components
- To remove impurities
- To obtain pure substances

**Methods of Separation**

**Handpicking**
- Used for large visible particles
- Separating stones from rice
- Removing unwanted particles

**Winnowing**
- Separating lighter particles from heavier ones
- Using wind or air
- Separating husk from grains

**Sieving**
- Separating particles of different sizes
- Using a sieve with holes
- Separating flour from bran

**Magnetic Separation**
- Separating magnetic substances from non-magnetic
- Using a magnet
- Separating iron filings from sand

**Sedimentation and Decantation**
- Sedimentation: heavier particles settle down
- Decantation: pouring off clear liquid
- Separating muddy water

**Filtration**
- Separating insoluble solid from liquid
- Using filter paper
- Examples: separating sand from water

**Evaporation**
- Obtaining dissolved solid from solution
- Heating the solution
- Liquid evaporates, solid remains
- Obtaining salt from sea water

**Condensation**
- Converting gas/vapor back to liquid
- Cooling the vapor
- Collecting pure water from salt water

## Theme 5: Elements and Compounds
### Content

**Elements**
- Simplest form of matter
- Made up of only one type of atom
- Cannot be broken down into simpler substances
- Basic building blocks of all matter

**Types of Elements**
- Metals: lustrous, good conductors, malleable, ductile
- Non-metals: dull, poor conductors, brittle
- Examples of metals: iron, copper, gold, silver, aluminium
- Examples of non-metals: oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, sulphur

**Symbols of Common Elements**
- Each element has a unique symbol
- Usually first one or two letters
- Examples: O for Oxygen, Fe for Iron, Cu for Copper

**Compounds**
- Formed by chemical combination of two or more elements
- Have fixed composition
- Have properties different from constituent elements
- Cannot be separated by physical methods

**Examples of Compounds**
- Water (H2O): hydrogen + oxygen
- Carbon dioxide (CO2): carbon + oxygen
- Common salt (NaCl): sodium + chlorine
- Sugar: carbon + hydrogen + oxygen

**Differences Between Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures**
- Elements: single type of atom
- Compounds: chemically combined elements
- Mixtures: physically combined substances

## Theme 6: Physical and Chemical Changes
### Content

**Physical Changes**
- No new substance is formed
- Only physical properties change
- Usually reversible
- No change in chemical composition

**Characteristics of Physical Changes**
- Change in shape, size, or state
- Original substance can be recovered
- No energy change (or very little)
- Temporary change

**Examples of Physical Changes**
- Melting of ice
- Dissolving sugar in water
- Tearing paper
- Breaking glass
- Stretching a rubber band
- Cutting vegetables

**Chemical Changes**
- New substance(s) formed
- Chemical composition changes
- Usually irreversible
- Also called chemical reactions

**Characteristics of Chemical Changes**
- Change in color
- Evolution of gas (bubbles)
- Change in temperature (heat/cold)
- Change in smell
- Formation of precipitate
- Emission of light

**Examples of Chemical Changes**
- Burning of paper or wood
- Rusting of iron
- Cooking food
- Curdling of milk
- Ripening of fruits
- Digestion of food
- Photosynthesis

**Differences Between Physical and Chemical Changes**
- New substance: no (physical) vs yes (chemical)
- Reversibility: usually reversible vs usually irreversible
- Energy change: little or none vs often significant
- Composition: unchanged vs changed

**Reversible and Irreversible Changes**
- Reversible: can go back to original
- Irreversible: cannot go back to original
- Examples of each type

## Theme 7: Air and Its Components
### Content

**Air is a Mixture**
- Air is a mixture of different gases
- Air is all around us
- Air is invisible but can be felt

**Composition of Air**
- Nitrogen: about 78%
- Oxygen: about 21%
- Carbon dioxide: about 0.03%
- Noble gases (argon, neon, etc.): about 0.97%
- Water vapor: variable amount
- Dust and smoke particles

**Properties of Oxygen**
- Colorless, odorless, tasteless gas
- Supports burning (combustion)
- Essential for respiration
- Slightly soluble in water

**Uses of Oxygen**
- Breathing and respiration
- Combustion and burning
- Medical purposes
- Welding and cutting metals

**Properties of Nitrogen**
- Colorless, odorless, tasteless gas
- Does not support burning
- Does not support respiration
- Less reactive than oxygen

**Uses of Nitrogen**
- Manufacturing of fertilizers
- Making ammonia
- Preserving food
- In electric bulbs

**Properties of Carbon Dioxide**
- Colorless, odorless gas
- Does not support burning
- Slightly heavier than air
- Dissolves in water to form weak acid

**Uses of Carbon Dioxide**
- Photosynthesis in plants
- Fire extinguishers
- Carbonated drinks (soda)
- Dry ice for cooling

**Air Pollution**
- Causes: vehicles, factories, burning
- Pollutants: smoke, dust, harmful gases
- Effects: health problems, global warming
- Prevention: reduce pollution, plant trees

## Theme 8: Water
### Content

**Importance of Water**
- Essential for all living things
- Makes up about 70% of human body
- Needed for all life processes
- Used in agriculture, industry, daily life

**Sources of Water**
- Rain water
- Surface water: rivers, lakes, ponds
- Groundwater: wells, springs
- Sea water (salty, not for drinking)

**Physical Properties of Water**
- Colorless, odorless, tasteless
- Exists in three states: solid (ice), liquid (water), gas (steam)
- Boiling point: 100 degrees Celsius
- Freezing point: 0 degrees Celsius
- High heat capacity

**Water Cycle**
- Evaporation: water to water vapor
- Condensation: water vapor to clouds
- Precipitation: rain, snow, hail
- Collection: rivers, lakes, oceans
- Continuous cycle in nature

**Water as a Solvent**
- Dissolves many substances
- Called universal solvent
- Forms solutions with soluble substances
- Cannot dissolve insoluble substances

**Types of Water**
- Fresh water: rivers, lakes, groundwater
- Sea water: salty, contains dissolved salts
- Potable water: safe for drinking

**Water Pollution**
- Causes: industrial waste, sewage, chemicals
- Effects: harm to aquatic life, diseases
- Sources: factories, households, agriculture

**Water Conservation**
- Importance of saving water
- Methods: rainwater harvesting, reducing waste
- Reusing and recycling water
- Fixing leaky taps and pipes

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 6 Chemistry, students should be able to:

- Define matter and list its characteristics
- Explain the particle nature of matter
- Describe the three states of matter and their properties
- Explain interconversion between states of matter
- Distinguish between pure substances and mixtures
- Identify homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures
- Apply appropriate separation techniques for different mixtures
- Define elements and compounds
- Differentiate between metals and non-metals
- Distinguish between physical and chemical changes
- Identify examples of physical and chemical changes
- List the components of air and their uses
- Explain the importance and properties of water
- Describe the water cycle
- Understand air and water pollution and conservation

## Assessment

### Components
- Written Examination: 80 marks
- Internal Assessment / Practical Work: 20 marks
- Total: 100 marks

### Examination Pattern
- Section A: Very Short Answer (MCQs and fill in the blanks) - 20 marks
- Section B: Short Answer (definitions, reasons, differences) - 30 marks
- Section C: Long Answer (detailed explanations, diagrams) - 30 marks

### Practical Activities
- Observing states of matter
- Interconversion of states (ice to water, water to steam)
- Preparing mixtures and solutions
- Separation of mixtures by various methods
- Identifying physical and chemical changes
- Testing presence of air and its components
- Water cycle demonstration
