# ICSE Grade 9 Chemistry Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Chemistry syllabus for Grade 9 builds upon foundational concepts from Grade 8 and introduces more advanced topics including atomic structure, chemical bonding, the periodic table, mole concept, and detailed study of important compounds. The curriculum emphasizes quantitative chemistry, systematic study of elements and compounds, and environmental awareness through practical laboratory work.

---

## Theme 1: Matter and Its Composition
### Content

**Physical Nature of Matter**
- Matter: anything that has mass and occupies space
- Kinetic particle theory review
- Arrangement of particles in solids, liquids, and gases
- Inter-particle forces and distances

**Interconversion of States**
- Melting and freezing
- Boiling and condensation
- Sublimation and deposition
- Energy changes during state changes

**Latent Heat**
- Latent heat of fusion: energy for solid to liquid change
- Latent heat of vaporization: energy for liquid to gas change
- Specific latent heat and calculations
- Heating and cooling curves

**Effects on States of Matter**
- Effect of temperature on particle motion
- Effect of pressure on boiling point
- Effect of pressure on melting point

**Diffusion and Brownian Motion**
- Diffusion: movement from high to low concentration
- Factors affecting diffusion
- Brownian motion as evidence of kinetic theory
- Random motion of particles

## Theme 2: Atomic Structure and Chemical Bonding
### Content

**Review of Atomic Structure**
- Bohr's atomic model: electrons in fixed orbits
- Energy levels: K, L, M, N shells
- Maximum electrons per shell: 2n²
- Atomic number and mass number
- Limitations of Bohr's model

**Electronic Configuration**
- Electronic configuration of elements 1-30
- Valence shell and valence electrons
- Relationship between electronic configuration and position in periodic table

**Octet and Duplet Rule**
- Octet rule: tendency to have 8 valence electrons
- Duplet rule for hydrogen and helium
- Noble gas configuration: stability

**Ionic Bonding**
- Formation by electron transfer
- Metal atoms lose electrons to form cations
- Non-metal atoms gain electrons to form anions
- Electrostatic attraction between ions
- Properties of ionic compounds:
  - High melting and boiling points
  - Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved
  - Usually soluble in water
  - Crystalline solids

**Covalent Bonding**
- Formation by electron sharing
- Shared pair of electrons
- Single, double, and triple bonds
- Electron dot (Lewis) structures
- Properties of covalent compounds:
  - Low melting and boiling points
  - Do not conduct electricity
  - Often insoluble in water

**Electrovalency and Covalency**
- Electrovalency: number of electrons transferred
- Covalency: number of electrons shared
- Examples and determination

## Theme 3: The Periodic Table
### Content

**Historical Development**
- Dobereiner's Triads:
  - Groups of three elements with similar properties
  - Middle element's atomic mass is average of other two
  - Limitations

- Newlands' Law of Octaves:
  - Every eighth element has similar properties
  - Like musical notes
  - Limitations

- Mendeleev's Periodic Table:
  - Arranged by increasing atomic mass
  - Predicted existence of unknown elements
  - Left gaps for undiscovered elements
  - Achievements and limitations

**Modern Periodic Law**
- Properties are periodic function of atomic number
- Moseley's contribution
- Long form of periodic table

**Structure of Modern Periodic Table**
- 7 periods (horizontal rows)
- 18 groups (vertical columns)
- Groups numbered 1-18 (IUPAC)
- Relationship with electronic configuration

**Blocks of Elements**
- s-block elements: Groups 1 and 2
- p-block elements: Groups 13-18
- d-block elements: Groups 3-12
- f-block elements: lanthanides and actinides

**Periodic Trends**
- Atomic radius:
  - Decreases across a period
  - Increases down a group

- Ionization energy:
  - Increases across a period
  - Decreases down a group

- Electronegativity:
  - Increases across a period
  - Decreases down a group

- Metallic and non-metallic character:
  - Metallic character decreases across period
  - Metallic character increases down group

## Theme 4: Chemical Equations and Balancing
### Content

**Types of Equations**
- Word equations: reactants and products in words
- Symbol equations: using chemical formulas
- Skeletal equations: unbalanced equations

**Law of Conservation of Mass**
- Matter cannot be created or destroyed
- Total mass of reactants equals total mass of products
- Basis for balancing equations

**Balancing Chemical Equations**
- Trial and error method (hit and trial)
- Systematic approach
- Coefficients vs subscripts
- Rules for balancing

**Types of Chemical Reactions**
- Combination/Synthesis: A + B → AB
- Decomposition: AB → A + B
- Single displacement: A + BC → AC + B
- Double displacement: AB + CD → AD + CB
- Neutralization: Acid + Base → Salt + Water

**Interpretation of Equations**
- In terms of atoms and molecules
- In terms of moles
- Mass relationships from equations

## Theme 5: The Mole Concept and Stoichiometry
### Content

**Relative Atomic Mass**
- Atomic mass unit (amu or u)
- Carbon-12 as standard
- Calculation of relative atomic mass

**Relative Molecular Mass**
- Sum of atomic masses in a molecule
- Formula mass for ionic compounds
- Calculation examples

**Avogadro's Number and the Mole**
- Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³
- Mole: amount containing Avogadro's number of particles
- SI unit of amount of substance

**Molar Mass**
- Mass of one mole of a substance
- Numerically equal to relative molecular mass
- Units: g/mol

**Mole Calculations**
- Moles = Mass/Molar mass
- Mass = Moles × Molar mass
- Number of particles = Moles × Avogadro's number

**Percentage Composition**
- Percentage of each element in a compound
- Calculation from formula
- Applications

**Empirical and Molecular Formula**
- Empirical formula: simplest whole number ratio
- Molecular formula: actual number of atoms
- Relationship: Molecular formula = n × Empirical formula
- Determination from percentage composition

## Theme 6: Acids, Bases, and Salts
### Content

**Definitions of Acids and Bases**
- Arrhenius theory:
  - Acids produce H⁺ ions in water
  - Bases produce OH⁻ ions in water

- Bronsted-Lowry theory:
  - Acids are proton donors
  - Bases are proton acceptors

**Properties of Acids**
- Sour taste
- Turn blue litmus red
- React with metals to produce hydrogen
- React with carbonates to produce CO₂
- React with bases (neutralization)

**Properties of Bases**
- Bitter taste
- Soapy/slippery feel
- Turn red litmus blue
- React with acids (neutralization)

**The pH Scale**
- pH range: 0 to 14
- pH 7: neutral
- pH < 7: acidic
- pH > 7: basic/alkaline
- Calculation concepts

**Indicators**
- Litmus: red/blue color change
- Methyl orange: red in acid, yellow in base
- Phenolphthalein: colorless in acid, pink in base
- Universal indicator: range of colors

**Strong and Weak Acids/Bases**
- Strong acids: complete dissociation (HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄)
- Weak acids: partial dissociation (CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃)
- Strong bases: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂
- Weak bases: NH₄OH, Mg(OH)₂

**Neutralization and Salts**
- Acid + Base → Salt + Water
- Types of salts:
  - Normal salts: all H⁺ replaced
  - Acidic salts: some H⁺ remain
  - Basic salts: excess base
- Examples of each type

## Theme 7: Study of First Element: Hydrogen
### Content

**Position in Periodic Table**
- Unique position: can be placed in Group 1 or 17
- Resemblance to alkali metals: +1 oxidation state
- Resemblance to halogens: forms H⁻ in hydrides
- Electronic configuration: 1s¹

**Isotopes of Hydrogen**
- Protium (¹H): 1 proton, 0 neutrons
- Deuterium (²H or D): 1 proton, 1 neutron
- Tritium (³H or T): 1 proton, 2 neutrons

**Laboratory Preparation**
- From dilute acid and metal:
  - Zn + H₂SO₄(dil) → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
  - Zn + 2HCl(dil) → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
- Collection by downward displacement of water
- Drying using concentrated H₂SO₄

**Industrial Manufacture**
- Electrolysis of acidified water
- Steam reforming of natural gas
- Bosch process from water gas

**Physical Properties**
- Colorless, odorless, tasteless gas
- Lightest gas known (density 0.09 g/L)
- Slightly soluble in water
- Neutral to litmus

**Chemical Properties**
- Combustion: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
- Reaction with halogens: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
- Reduction of metal oxides: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
- Hydrogenation of oils

**Tests for Hydrogen**
- Burns with pop sound when a burning splint is applied

**Uses of Hydrogen**
- Fuel cells (clean energy)
- Manufacture of ammonia (Haber process)
- Hydrogenation of oils
- Reducing agent in metallurgy

**Oxidation and Reduction**
- Hydrogen as a reducing agent
- Oxide reduction reactions

## Theme 8: Study of Water
### Content

**Structure of Water Molecule**
- Molecular formula: H₂O
- Bent/angular shape
- Bond angle: 104.5°
- Polar molecule

**Hydrogen Bonding**
- Intermolecular hydrogen bonds
- Responsible for high boiling point
- Responsible for anomalous expansion

**Anomalous Behavior of Water**
- Maximum density at 4°C
- Expansion on freezing (ice floats)
- Importance for aquatic life
- Comparison with normal substances

**Water as a Solvent**
- Universal solvent properties
- Polar and ionic substances dissolve well
- Concentration terms: molarity, percentage
- Saturated, unsaturated, supersaturated solutions

**Chemical Properties**
- Reaction with metals: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
- Reaction with non-metals (hot carbon): C + H₂O → CO + H₂
- Reaction with metal oxides: forming bases
- Reaction with non-metal oxides: forming acids
- Electrolysis: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

**Hard and Soft Water**
- Soft water: lathers easily with soap
- Hard water: does not lather easily
- Temporary hardness: Ca(HCO₃)₂, Mg(HCO₃)₂
- Permanent hardness: CaSO₄, MgSO₄, CaCl₂

**Removal of Hardness**
- Temporary hardness:
  - Boiling: Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂
  - Clark's process: adding slaked lime
- Permanent hardness:
  - Adding washing soda (Na₂CO₃)
  - Ion exchange method
  - Distillation

## Theme 9: Atmospheric Pollution
### Content

**Composition of Atmosphere**
- Nitrogen: 78%
- Oxygen: 21%
- Argon: 0.93%
- Carbon dioxide: 0.04%
- Other gases: trace amounts

**Air Pollutants**
- Primary pollutants: directly emitted
  - Carbon monoxide (CO)
  - Sulfur dioxide (SO₂)
  - Nitrogen oxides (NOₓ)
  - Particulate matter

- Secondary pollutants: formed by reactions
  - Ozone (ground level)
  - Photochemical smog

**Sources of Pollution**
- Vehicles: CO, NOₓ, hydrocarbons
- Industries: SO₂, particulates
- Power plants: SO₂, CO₂, NOₓ
- Burning: smoke, particulates

**Greenhouse Effect**
- Greenhouse gases: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, CFCs, water vapor
- Mechanism: trap infrared radiation
- Natural vs enhanced greenhouse effect

**Global Warming**
- Causes: increased greenhouse gases
- Effects:
  - Rising temperatures
  - Melting ice caps
  - Rising sea levels
  - Climate change
- Mitigation measures

**Ozone Layer Depletion**
- Ozone (O₃) in stratosphere
- Protection from UV radiation
- CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) as cause
- Ozone hole
- Montreal Protocol

**Acid Rain**
- Formation:
  - SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (sulfurous acid)
  - 2SO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 2H₂SO₄
  - NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃ + HNO₂
- Effects on:
  - Buildings and monuments
  - Aquatic life
  - Forests
  - Soil

**Pollution Control**
- Catalytic converters in vehicles
- Electrostatic precipitators
- Scrubbers in chimneys
- Alternative fuels
- Afforestation

## Theme 11: Study of Compounds: Ammonia and Nitric Acid
### Content

**Ammonia (NH₃)**

*Laboratory Preparation*
- Heating ammonium salt with alkali:
  - 2NH₄Cl + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCl₂ + 2H₂O + 2NH₃↑
  - NH₄Cl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃↑
- Collection by downward displacement of air (less dense than air)
- Drying using quicklime (CaO)

*Industrial Manufacture*
- Haber process: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃
- Conditions: 450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst

*Physical Properties*
- Colorless gas with pungent smell
- Lighter than air
- Extremely soluble in water (1:700 at STP)
- Easily liquefied

*Chemical Properties*
- Basic gas: forms NH₄OH in water
- Reaction with acids: NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl (white fumes)
- Fountain experiment: demonstration of high solubility
- Combustion: 4NH₃ + 3O₂ → 2N₂ + 6H₂O
- Reducing action

*Uses of Ammonia*
- Manufacturing fertilizers
- Manufacturing nitric acid
- Refrigerant
- Cleaning agent
- Manufacture of explosives

**Nitric Acid (HNO₃)**

*Laboratory Preparation*
- From nitrate + conc. H₂SO₄:
  - NaNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → NaHSO₄ + HNO₃

*Industrial Manufacture*
- Ostwald process:
  - 4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O (Pt catalyst, 800°C)
  - 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂
  - 4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃

*Properties of Nitric Acid*
- Colorless liquid (yellow when impure)
- Pungent smell
- Strong acid and oxidizing agent
- Reacts with most metals except Au, Pt
- Brown fumes (NO₂) with copper

*Uses of Nitric Acid*
- Manufacturing fertilizers
- Manufacturing explosives
- Manufacturing dyes
- Etching metals

## Theme 12: Study of Compounds: Hydrochloric Acid and Sulphuric Acid
### Content

**Hydrochloric Acid (HCl)**

*Laboratory Preparation*
- NaCl + H₂SO₄ → NaHSO₄ + HCl↑
- Collection by downward displacement of air

*Properties*
- Colorless, pungent-smelling solution
- Strong acid, completely dissociates
- Monobasic acid

*Reactions*
- With metals: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
- With bases: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
- With carbonates: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
- With AgNO₃: white precipitate of AgCl

*Uses*
- Pickling of metals
- Manufacturing chlorides
- Cleaning agents
- Food processing

**Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)**

*Industrial Manufacture (Contact Process)*
- S + O₂ → SO₂
- 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (V₂O₅ catalyst, 450°C)
- SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum)
- H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄

*Properties of Concentrated H₂SO₄*
- Dense, oily liquid
- Strong dehydrating agent
- Chars organic matter
- Oxidizing agent when hot

*Properties of Dilute H₂SO₄*
- Typical acid properties
- Dibasic acid
- Reactions with metals, bases, carbonates

*Reactions*
- With metals: Zn + H₂SO₄(dil) → ZnSO₄ + H₂
- With bases: H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
- Dehydration: C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ → 12C + 11H₂O (with conc. acid)

*Dilution Safety*
- Always add acid to water
- Never add water to acid
- Highly exothermic reaction

*Uses*
- Lead-acid batteries
- Manufacturing fertilizers
- Manufacturing detergents
- Petroleum refining
- Manufacturing explosives

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 9 Chemistry, students should be able to:

- Explain matter composition and state interconversions quantitatively
- Describe atomic structure and write electronic configurations
- Explain ionic and covalent bonding with examples
- Describe the modern periodic table and periodic trends
- Balance chemical equations and perform stoichiometric calculations
- Apply the mole concept to solve numerical problems
- Explain acid-base reactions and pH scale
- Describe preparation, properties, and uses of hydrogen and water
- Analyze causes and effects of atmospheric pollution
- Perform laboratory experiments safely and accurately
- Describe preparation and properties of ammonia and nitric acid
- Describe preparation and properties of HCl and H₂SO₄

- Written Examination: 80 marks

- Section A: Very Short Answer (MCQs, fill in blanks) - 20 marks
- Section B: Short Answer (equations, calculations, reasoning) - 30 marks
- Section C: Long Answer (detailed explanations, numericals) - 30 marks

- Preparation and tests for gases (H₂, O₂, CO₂, NH₃)
- Acid-base titrations (simple)
- Crystallization experiments
- Study of action of acids on metals
- Determination of pH using indicators
- Qualitative tests for ions
