# ICSE Grade 10 Physics Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Physics syllabus for Grade 10 is the Board examination syllabus that builds comprehensively on concepts from earlier grades. It covers Force and Equilibrium, Work-Energy-Power, Simple Machines, Light (Refraction and Lenses), Sound, Current Electricity, Electromagnetism, Calorimetry, and Radioactivity. The curriculum emphasizes conceptual understanding, mathematical problem-solving, and real-world applications, preparing students for higher secondary physics.

---

## Unit 1: Force
### Content

**Turning Effect of Force (Moment of Force)**
- Moment of a force about a point: definition and calculation
- Moment = Force x Perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation
- SI unit of moment: Newton-meter (Nm)
- Clockwise and anticlockwise moments
- Couple: two equal and opposite parallel forces not acting along the same line
- Moment of a couple = Force x Perpendicular distance between the forces
- Couples in everyday life: steering wheel, opening a bottle cap

**Principle of Moments and Equilibrium**
- Principle of moments: for a body in rotational equilibrium, sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments
- Conditions for equilibrium of a rigid body:
  - Net force = 0 (translational equilibrium)
  - Net moment = 0 (rotational equilibrium)
- Verification of the principle of moments using a metre rule

**Centre of Gravity**
- Centre of gravity: the point through which the entire weight of a body appears to act
- Centre of gravity of regular bodies (uniform laminae)
- Determining centre of gravity of irregular laminae by the plumb line method
- Uniform and non-uniform bodies

**Stability**
- Types of equilibrium: stable, unstable, and neutral
- Factors affecting stability: position of centre of gravity, area of base
- Applications: design of vehicles, buildings, furniture

## Unit 2: Work, Energy and Power
### Content

**Work**
- Work done by a constant force
- W = F x s x cos(theta), where theta is the angle between force and displacement
- Positive work, negative work, and zero work
- SI unit of work: Joule (J)
- Conditions for work to be done

**Energy**
- Energy as the capacity to do work
- Forms of energy: kinetic, potential, heat, light, sound, electrical, chemical, nuclear
- Mechanical energy = Kinetic energy + Potential energy

**Kinetic Energy**
- Energy of a body due to its motion
- KE = (1/2)mv^2
- Derivation of kinetic energy formula using work-energy theorem
- Kinetic energy depends on mass and velocity (squared)

**Potential Energy**
- Energy of a body due to its position or configuration
- Gravitational potential energy: PE = mgh
- Reference point for potential energy
- Elastic potential energy (concept)

**Conservation of Energy**
- Law of conservation of energy: energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed
- Conservation of mechanical energy in ideal systems (no friction)
- Energy transformations in various systems: pendulum, falling objects, roller coaster

**Power**
- Rate of doing work or rate of energy transfer
- P = W/t = F x v
- SI unit of power: Watt (W)
- Commercial unit: horsepower (hp), 1 hp = 746 W
- Efficiency = (Useful output energy / Total input energy) x 100%
- Efficiency = (MA / VR) x 100%

## Unit 3: Simple Machines
### Content

**Machines: Basic Concepts**
- Machine: a device that makes work easier by modifying force, distance, or direction
- Mechanical Advantage (MA) = Load / Effort
- Velocity Ratio (VR) = Distance moved by effort / Distance moved by load
- Efficiency = (MA / VR) x 100%
- Ideal machine vs real machine
- Energy losses in machines

**Levers**
- Lever: a rigid bar that can rotate about a fixed point (fulcrum)
- First class lever: fulcrum between load and effort (e.g., see-saw, scissors)
- Second class lever: load between fulcrum and effort (e.g., wheelbarrow, nutcracker)
- Third class lever: effort between fulcrum and load (e.g., fishing rod, tweezers)
- MA and VR of levers
- Practical applications

**Pulleys**
- Single fixed pulley: changes direction of force, VR = 1
- Single movable pulley: multiplies force, VR = 2
- Combination of pulleys: block and tackle system
- VR of a pulley system = number of rope segments supporting the load
- Practical applications: cranes, lifts, wells

**Inclined Plane**
- Inclined plane: a sloping surface used to raise a load
- VR = Length of inclined plane / Vertical height
- MA = Load / Effort
- Applications: ramps, staircases, wedges

**Screw**
- Screw: an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder
- Pitch: distance between consecutive threads
- VR = 2(pi)R / Pitch, where R is the radius of the effort arm
- Applications: screw jack, nut and bolt

## Unit 4: Light
### Content

**Refraction at Plane Surfaces**
- Refraction: bending of light as it passes from one medium to another
- Cause of refraction: change in speed of light
- Laws of refraction:
  - Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane
  - Snell's law: sin i / sin r = constant = refractive index
- Refractive index (n): ratio of speed of light in vacuum to speed in medium
- Absolute and relative refractive index
- Optically denser and rarer media
- Refraction through a rectangular glass slab: lateral displacement
- Numerical problems on Snell's law

**Critical Angle and Total Internal Reflection**
- Critical angle: angle of incidence for which angle of refraction is 90 degrees
- Condition for total internal reflection:
  - Light travels from denser to rarer medium
  - Angle of incidence > critical angle
- Relation: sin(c) = 1/n
- Applications: optical fibers, prisms in binoculars, diamond sparkle, mirages

**Refraction Through a Prism**
- Prism: a transparent medium bounded by plane surfaces inclined to each other
- Path of light through a prism
- Angle of deviation
- Factors affecting angle of deviation: angle of incidence, refractive index, prism angle
- Minimum deviation
- Dispersion of light: splitting of white light into constituent colors
- Spectrum: VIBGYOR
- Cause of dispersion: different colors have different speeds in glass
- Recombination of spectrum using another prism

**Refraction Through Lenses**
- Types of lenses: convex (converging) and concave (diverging)
- Terms: optical centre, principal axis, principal focus, focal length
- Rules for ray diagrams
- Image formation by convex lens for objects at different positions
- Image formation by concave lens
- Nature, position, and size of images
- Applications: magnifying glass, camera, projector

**Lens Formula and Magnification**
- Lens formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u
- Sign convention for lenses (New Cartesian Sign Convention)
- Magnification: m = v/u = h'/h (image height / object height)
- Numerical problems on lens formula

**Power of a Lens**
- Power = 1 / focal length (in metres)
- SI unit: Dioptre (D)
- Power of convex lens is positive, concave lens is negative
- Power of combination of thin lenses in contact: P = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...

**Defects of Vision and Correction**
- Structure of the human eye: cornea, pupil, iris, eye lens, retina
- Near point (least distance of distinct vision): 25 cm for normal eye
- Far point: farthest distance of clear vision (infinity for normal eye)
- Power of accommodation

**Myopia (Short-sightedness)**
- Cause: elongated eyeball or excessive curvature of eye lens
- Far point is finite (not at infinity)
- Image forms in front of retina
- Correction: concave (diverging) lens
- Numerical problems on corrective lens power

**Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness)**
- Cause: shortened eyeball or weak eye lens
- Near point is beyond 25 cm
- Image forms behind retina
- Correction: convex (converging) lens
- Numerical problems on corrective lens power

**Presbyopia**
- Age-related loss of accommodation
- Both near and far vision affected
- Correction: bifocal lenses

## Unit 5: Sound
### Content

**Nature of Sound Waves**
- Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave
- Requires a medium for propagation
- Compression and rarefaction
- Sound cannot travel through vacuum

**Wave Parameters**
- Frequency (f): number of vibrations per second, SI unit: Hertz (Hz)
- Time period (T): time for one complete vibration, T = 1/f
- Wavelength (lambda): distance between consecutive compressions or rarefactions
- Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from mean position
- Wave velocity: v = f x lambda
- Speed of sound in air at 20 degrees C: approximately 344 m/s
- Speed of sound: solid > liquid > gas

**Characteristics of Sound**
- Loudness: depends on amplitude and distance from source
- Intensity: energy per unit area per unit time, SI unit: W/m^2
- Decibel (dB) scale for measuring loudness
- Pitch: depends on frequency (high frequency = high pitch)
- Quality (timbre): distinguishes sounds of same pitch and loudness from different sources

**Reflection of Sound**
- Sound follows laws of reflection similar to light
- Applications: speaking tubes, megaphones, sound boards

**Echo**
- Echo: distinct repetition of sound due to reflection
- Condition for hearing an echo: time gap >= 0.1 s
- Minimum distance for echo = v x t / 2 = 17.2 m (at 20 degrees C)
- Reverberation: prolonged sound due to multiple reflections
- Control of reverberation: sound absorbing materials

**Ultrasound**
- Ultrasound: sound waves with frequency > 20,000 Hz (above audible range)
- Audible range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
- Infrasound: frequency < 20 Hz
- Applications of ultrasound:
  - SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging)
  - Medical imaging (ultrasonography)
  - Cleaning delicate instruments
  - Detecting cracks in metals

## Unit 6: Current Electricity
### Content

**Electric Current**
- Electric current: rate of flow of electric charge
- I = Q/t, SI unit: Ampere (A)
- Direction of current: conventional current (positive to negative) vs electron flow
- Ammeter: instrument to measure current (connected in series)

**Potential Difference and EMF**
- Potential difference (V): work done per unit charge in moving charge between two points
- V = W/Q, SI unit: Volt (V)
- Voltmeter: instrument to measure potential difference (connected in parallel)
- Electromotive force (EMF): energy supplied per unit charge by a source
- Difference between EMF and terminal potential difference

**Ohm's Law**
- Ohm's law: V = IR (at constant temperature for ohmic conductors)
- Resistance (R): opposition to current flow
- SI unit of resistance: Ohm (omega)
- I-V characteristics: ohmic vs non-ohmic conductors
- Verification of Ohm's law

**Factors Affecting Resistance**
- Length: R directly proportional to L
- Cross-sectional area: R inversely proportional to A
- Material: different materials have different resistivities
- Temperature: resistance of metals increases with temperature
- Formula: R = rho x L / A
- Resistivity (rho): material constant, SI unit: Ohm-metre

**Resistors in Series**
- Same current flows through all resistors
- Total voltage = sum of individual voltages
- Equivalent resistance: Rs = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
- Voltage divider principle

**Resistors in Parallel**
- Same voltage across all resistors
- Total current = sum of individual currents
- Equivalent resistance: 1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...
- Parallel equivalent is always less than smallest individual resistance

**Internal Resistance of a Cell**
- Internal resistance (r): resistance offered by electrolyte of cell
- EMF (E) = Terminal voltage (V) + Voltage drop across internal resistance (Ir)
- E = V + Ir or V = E - Ir
- Current in circuit: I = E / (R + r)

**Electrical Energy and Power**
- Electrical energy: W = VIt = I^2Rt = V^2t/R
- SI unit: Joule (J)
- Electrical power: P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R
- SI unit: Watt (W)
- Commercial unit of energy: kilowatt-hour (kWh)
- 1 kWh = 3.6 x 10^6 J
- Calculation of electricity bills

**Heating Effect of Current**
- Joule's law of heating: H = I^2Rt
- Applications: electric heater, electric iron, fuse, electric bulb
- Fuse: safety device that melts when current exceeds safe limit

**Household Circuits**
- Domestic wiring: live (phase), neutral, earth
- Ring main circuit
- Earthing: safety measure connecting appliances to ground
- Fuses and Miniature Circuit Breakers (MCB)
- Power rating of appliances
- Safety precautions with electricity

## Unit 7: Electromagnetism
### Content

**Magnetic Effect of Electric Current**
- Electric current produces magnetic field
- Oersted's experiment
- Right-hand thumb rule: direction of magnetic field around straight conductor
- Maxwell's corkscrew rule
- Magnetic field patterns:
  - Straight current-carrying conductor: concentric circles
  - Circular loop: straight through center
  - Solenoid: similar to bar magnet

**Electromagnets**
- Electromagnet: temporary magnet created by passing current through a coil
- Increasing strength: more turns, more current, soft iron core
- Applications: electric bell, electromagnetic relay, crane, loudspeaker

**Force on Current-Carrying Conductor**
- A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force
- F = BIL (for perpendicular orientation)
- B: magnetic field strength, I: current, L: length of conductor
- Fleming's left-hand rule: direction of force

**DC Motor**
- Principle: force on current-carrying conductor in magnetic field
- Construction: armature coil, permanent magnet, split ring commutator, brushes
- Working: coil rotates due to force, commutator reverses current direction each half rotation
- Applications: electric fan, mixer, washing machine

**Electromagnetic Induction**
- Faraday's experiments: moving magnet induces current in coil
- Electromagnetic induction: production of EMF (and current) due to changing magnetic flux
- Factors affecting induced EMF: rate of change of flux, number of turns
- Direction of induced current: Fleming's right-hand rule
- Lenz's law: induced current opposes the change producing it

**AC Generator**
- Principle: electromagnetic induction
- Construction: armature coil, permanent magnet, slip rings, brushes
- Working: rotation of coil changes magnetic flux, inducing alternating EMF
- AC vs DC: alternating current changes direction periodically
- Frequency of AC in India: 50 Hz

**Transformers**
- Transformer: device to change voltage of AC
- Principle: mutual electromagnetic induction
- Construction: primary coil, secondary coil, laminated soft iron core
- Step-up transformer: Vs > Vp (Ns > Np)
- Step-down transformer: Vs < Vp (Ns < Np)
- Transformer equation: Vs/Vp = Ns/Np = Ip/Is
- For ideal transformer: VpIp = VsIs (power conserved)
- Power transmission: high voltage reduces power loss (P = I^2R)
- Applications: power transmission, chargers, adapters

## Unit 8: Calorimetry
### Content

**Heat and Temperature**
- Heat: energy transferred due to temperature difference
- Temperature: measure of average kinetic energy of molecules
- Heat capacity (C): heat required to raise temperature by 1 degree
- C = Q / delta_T, SI unit: J/K or J/degree C

**Specific Heat Capacity**
- Specific heat capacity (c): heat required to raise 1 kg by 1 degree
- Q = mc x delta_T
- SI unit: J/(kg.K) or J/(kg.degree C)
- Specific heat of water: 4200 J/(kg.K) or 4.2 J/(g.degree C)
- High specific heat of water: significance and applications

**Principle of Calorimetry**
- Calorimetry: measurement of heat
- Principle: heat lost by hot body = heat gained by cold body (in isolated system)
- Method of mixtures for determining specific heat
- Numerical problems on heat exchange

**Latent Heat**
- Latent heat: heat absorbed or released during change of state at constant temperature
- Latent heat of fusion (Lf): heat to convert unit mass of solid to liquid at melting point
- Q = mLf
- Latent heat of fusion of ice: 336 kJ/kg or 80 cal/g
- Latent heat of vaporization (Lv): heat to convert unit mass of liquid to vapor at boiling point
- Q = mLv
- Latent heat of vaporization of water: 2260 kJ/kg or 540 cal/g
- SI unit of latent heat: J/kg

**Heating and Cooling Curves**
- Temperature vs time graph during heating/cooling
- Horizontal portions indicate change of state
- Numerical problems involving specific heat and latent heat

## Unit 9: Radioactivity
### Content

**Structure of Atom**
- Atom: protons (positive), neutrons (neutral), electrons (negative)
- Nucleus: contains protons and neutrons (nucleons)
- Atomic number (Z): number of protons
- Mass number (A): number of protons + neutrons
- Isotopes: atoms with same Z but different A

**Radioactivity**
- Radioactivity: spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei
- Discovered by Henri Becquerel (1896)
- Radioactive elements: uranium, radium, thorium, etc.
- Radioactive decay: unstable nuclei emit radiation to become stable

**Types of Radioactive Emissions**
- Alpha particles (alpha):
  - Helium nuclei (2 protons, 2 neutrons)
  - Positive charge (+2e)
  - High ionizing power, low penetrating power
  - Stopped by paper or skin
  - A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2
- Beta particles (beta):
  - High-speed electrons
  - Negative charge (-e)
  - Moderate ionizing and penetrating power
  - Stopped by aluminum sheet
  - A unchanged, Z increases by 1
- Gamma rays (gamma):
  - Electromagnetic radiation (photons)
  - No charge, no mass
  - Low ionizing power, high penetrating power
  - Stopped by thick lead or concrete

**Half-Life**
- Half-life (T1/2): time taken for half the radioactive nuclei to decay
- Characteristic of each radioactive element
- Applications: carbon dating, medical diagnosis

**Nuclear Fission**
- Fission: splitting of heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei
- Induced by neutron bombardment (e.g., U-235)
- Releases enormous energy and more neutrons
- Chain reaction: self-sustaining fission
- Critical mass: minimum mass for sustained chain reaction
- Controlled fission: nuclear reactor
- Uncontrolled fission: atomic bomb

**Nuclear Fusion**
- Fusion: combining of light nuclei to form heavier nucleus
- Requires extremely high temperature and pressure
- Source of energy in stars (including Sun)
- Hydrogen fusion to helium
- Releases more energy per unit mass than fission
- Thermonuclear reactions

**Nuclear Energy: Applications and Hazards**
- Nuclear power plants: controlled fission for electricity generation
- Nuclear reactor components: fuel rods, moderator, control rods, coolant
- Medical applications: cancer treatment, imaging (radioactive tracers)
- Carbon dating: archaeological and geological applications
- Industrial applications: thickness measurement, sterilization
- Hazards: radiation sickness, genetic mutations, cancer
- Nuclear waste: long-term storage problem
- Safety measures: shielding, monitoring, waste management

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 10 Physics, students should be able to:

- Calculate moments of forces and apply the principle of moments for equilibrium
- Solve numerical problems on work, energy, power, and efficiency
- Analyze simple machines using MA, VR, and efficiency
- Apply Snell's law and calculate refractive index, critical angle
- Draw ray diagrams and use lens formula for image formation
- Explain defects of vision and calculate power of corrective lenses
- Describe characteristics of sound and calculate echo distances
- Apply Ohm's law and solve circuit problems with series-parallel combinations
- Calculate electrical energy, power, and electricity bills
- Explain working of DC motor, AC generator, and transformer
- Solve numerical problems on calorimetry using specific heat and latent heat
- Describe radioactivity, nuclear reactions, and their applications

## Assessment

### Components
- Written Examination: 80 marks
- Internal Assessment / Practical Work: 20 marks
- Total: 100 marks

### Examination Pattern
- Section A: Compulsory short answer questions covering entire syllabus (40 marks)
- Section B: Answer six questions out of nine, choice-based (60 marks)

### Types of Questions
- Very short answer (1-2 marks): definitions, units, one-word answers
- Short answer (3-4 marks): brief explanations, simple calculations
- Long answer (5-8 marks): detailed explanations, diagrams, numerical problems

### Practical Work
- Verification of principle of moments
- Determination of focal length of convex lens
- Determination of specific heat capacity
- Study of Ohm's law
- Verification of laws of reflection of sound
