# ICSE Grade 9 Physics Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Physics syllabus for Grade 9 builds upon the foundational concepts from Grade 8 and introduces more advanced topics with mathematical rigor. The curriculum emphasizes quantitative problem-solving, derivations, and practical applications of physical principles. This year marks the transition to secondary-level physics with detailed study of motion, forces, energy, optics, and electricity.

---

## Theme 1: Measurements and Experimentation
### Content

**Physical Quantities and Measurements**
- Physical quantities: base and derived
- Seven SI base units: metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela
- Derived units: Newton, Joule, Pascal, Watt
- Prefixes: nano, micro, milli, centi, kilo, mega

**Vernier Caliper**
- Construction and parts
- Main scale and vernier scale readings
- Least count: 0.1 mm or 0.01 cm
- Taking measurements of length, external diameter, internal diameter

**Screw Gauge (Micrometer)**
- Construction and parts
- Main scale and circular scale readings
- Least count: 0.01 mm
- Measuring small lengths and diameters

**Zero Error and Correction**
- Positive zero error
- Negative zero error
- Correction of readings

**Accuracy and Precision**
- Significant figures: rules for counting
- Rounding off numbers
- Difference between accuracy and precision

## Theme 2: Motion in One Dimension
### Content

**Distance and Displacement**
- Distance: total path length (scalar quantity)
- Displacement: shortest distance between two points (vector quantity)
- SI unit: metre (m)

**Speed and Velocity**
- Speed: rate of change of distance (scalar)
- Velocity: rate of change of displacement (vector)
- Average speed and average velocity
- Instantaneous speed and velocity
- SI unit: m/s

**Uniform and Non-uniform Motion**
- Uniform motion: constant velocity
- Non-uniform motion: changing velocity
- Examples of each type

**Acceleration**
- Definition: rate of change of velocity
- Formula: a = (v - u) / t
- SI unit: m/s^2
- Positive acceleration (speeding up) and negative acceleration/deceleration (slowing down)

**Equations of Motion**
- First equation: v = u + at
- Second equation: s = ut + (1/2)at^2
- Third equation: v^2 = u^2 + 2as
- Derivation and applications

**Graphical Representation of Motion**
- Distance-time graphs
- Velocity-time graphs
- Calculating velocity from slope of s-t graph
- Calculating distance from area under v-t graph
- Calculating acceleration from slope of v-t graph

## Theme 3: Laws of Motion
### Content

**Newton's First Law of Motion**
- Statement: A body at rest stays at rest and a body in motion continues in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force
- Inertia: tendency to resist change in state of motion
- Types: inertia of rest, inertia of motion, inertia of direction
- Mass as a measure of inertia

**Newton's Second Law of Motion**
- Momentum: p = mv, SI unit: kg m/s
- Rate of change of momentum equals applied force
- Force = mass x acceleration (F = ma)
- SI unit of force: Newton (N)
- 1 N = 1 kg m/s^2

**Newton's Third Law of Motion**
- Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction
- Action and reaction act on different bodies
- Examples: walking, swimming, rocket propulsion, recoil of gun

**Conservation of Momentum**
- Law of conservation of linear momentum
- Total momentum before = Total momentum after (in absence of external force)
- Applications: collisions, explosions, recoil

**Friction**
- Definition: force opposing relative motion
- Types: static friction, sliding (kinetic) friction, rolling friction
- Static friction > Sliding friction > Rolling friction
- Factors affecting friction: nature of surfaces, normal force
- Advantages and disadvantages of friction
- Methods to reduce and increase friction

## Theme 4: Gravitation
### Content

**Newton's Law of Gravitation**
- Universal law of gravitation: F = Gm1m2/r^2
- G = 6.67 x 10^-11 N m^2/kg^2
- Properties of gravitational force: universal, always attractive, central force

**Acceleration due to Gravity**
- Definition: acceleration of freely falling body
- Formula: g = GM/R^2
- Value: g = 9.8 m/s^2 (approximately 10 m/s^2 for calculations)
- g is same for all objects regardless of mass

**Mass and Weight**
- Mass: amount of matter in a body (scalar, constant everywhere)
- Weight: gravitational force on a body (W = mg)
- Weight varies with location
- SI units: mass in kg, weight in N

**Free Fall**
- Motion under gravity alone
- Air resistance neglected
- Equations of motion with g replacing a
- Objects in free fall experience weightlessness

**Variation of g**
- g decreases with altitude: g' = g(1 - 2h/R) for small h
- g decreases with depth: g' = g(1 - d/R)
- g is maximum at poles and minimum at equator

## Theme 5: Fluids
### Content

**Pressure in Fluids**
- Pressure = Force / Area
- Pressure in a liquid: P = rho x g x h
- Pressure at a point acts equally in all directions
- SI unit: Pascal (Pa)

**Pascal's Law**
- Statement: Pressure applied to enclosed fluid is transmitted equally
- Hydraulic machines: lift, press, brakes
- Mechanical advantage: A2/A1 = F2/F1

**Atmospheric Pressure**
- Standard value: 1 atm = 101325 Pa = 760 mm Hg
- Mercury barometer: principle and measurement
- Aneroid barometer
- Manometer: U-tube manometer, open and closed types

**Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle**
- Buoyant force: upward force on submerged object
- Archimedes' principle: Upthrust = Weight of displaced fluid
- Upthrust = rho x V x g
- Apparent weight = True weight - Upthrust

**Law of Floatation**
- A floating body displaces its own weight of fluid
- Conditions: floating (density < fluid), sinking (density > fluid), neutral (density = fluid)
- Applications: ships, submarines, balloons

**Relative Density and Hydrometers**
- Relative density = Density of substance / Density of water
- RD has no unit
- Hydrometer: principle and use for measuring RD of liquids

## Theme 6: Work, Energy, and Power
### Content

**Work**
- Work = Force x Displacement x cos(theta)
- Work done when force causes displacement
- Positive work: force and displacement in same direction
- Negative work: force and displacement in opposite directions
- Zero work: force perpendicular to displacement
- SI unit: Joule (J)

**Energy**
- Definition: capacity to do work
- SI unit: Joule (J)
- Kinetic energy: KE = (1/2)mv^2
- Potential energy: PE = mgh (gravitational)
- Mechanical energy = KE + PE

**Work-Energy Theorem**
- Work done on a body = Change in kinetic energy
- W = (1/2)mv^2 - (1/2)mu^2

**Conservation of Mechanical Energy**
- Total mechanical energy remains constant (when no friction)
- Energy converts between KE and PE
- Examples: pendulum, falling objects, roller coaster

**Power**
- Definition: rate of doing work
- Power = Work / Time = Energy / Time
- Power = Force x Velocity
- SI unit: Watt (W)
- 1 kW = 1000 W, 1 MW = 10^6 W

**Efficiency**
- Efficiency = (Useful output / Total input) x 100%
- Efficiency < 100% due to friction and other losses
- Commercial unit of energy: kWh
- 1 kWh = 3.6 x 10^6 J

## Theme 7: Simple Machines
### Content

**Basic Concepts**
- Simple machine: device that makes work easier
- Effort (E): force applied by user
- Load (L): resistance to be overcome
- Mechanical Advantage: MA = Load / Effort
- Velocity Ratio: VR = Distance moved by effort / Distance moved by load
- Efficiency = (MA / VR) x 100%

**Levers**
- Lever: rigid bar pivoting about fulcrum
- First class lever: fulcrum between load and effort
- Second class lever: load between fulcrum and effort
- Third class lever: effort between fulcrum and load
- MA of lever = Effort arm / Load arm
- Examples and applications of each class

**Pulleys**
- Single fixed pulley: VR = 1, changes direction only
- Single movable pulley: VR = 2
- Block and tackle system: VR = number of strings supporting load
- Calculating MA and efficiency of pulley systems

**Inclined Plane**
- VR = Length of plane / Height
- Mechanical advantage
- Related machines: wedge (double inclined plane), screw
- Screw: VR = 2(pi)r / pitch

## Theme 8: Heat and Calorimetry
### Content

**Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity**
- Heat capacity (C): heat required to raise temperature by 1 degree
- C = Q / delta T, unit: J/K or J/C
- Specific heat capacity (c): heat per unit mass per degree
- c = Q / (m x delta T)
- SI unit: J/(kg.K) or J/(kg.C)
- Specific heat of water: 4200 J/(kg.K)
- Specific heat of common substances

**Principle of Calorimetry**
- Heat lost by hot body = Heat gained by cold body
- m1 x c1 x (T1 - Tf) = m2 x c2 x (Tf - T2)
- Calorimeter: construction and use
- Method of mixtures for finding specific heat

**Latent Heat**
- Heat absorbed or released without temperature change
- Specific latent heat of fusion (Lf): Q = mLf
- Lf of ice = 336 kJ/kg or 80 cal/g
- Specific latent heat of vaporization (Lv): Q = mLv
- Lv of water = 2260 kJ/kg or 540 cal/g

**Change of State**
- Heating curve: temperature vs heat supplied
- Cooling curve: temperature vs time
- Temperature remains constant during phase change
- Why burns from steam are worse than boiling water

## Theme 9: Light
### Content

**Reflection of Light**
- Laws of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection
- Both angles measured from normal
- Regular and diffuse reflection
- Plane mirrors: image characteristics

**Spherical Mirrors**
- Concave and convex mirrors
- Terms: pole, center of curvature, principal axis, focus, focal length
- Relationship: R = 2f
- Mirror formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u
- Sign convention (New Cartesian)
- Magnification: m = -v/u = h'/h
- Image formation at different object positions
- Uses of concave and convex mirrors

**Refraction of Light**
- Laws of refraction (Snell's law): n1 sin i = n2 sin r
- Refractive index: n = c/v = sin i / sin r
- Relative refractive index: n21 = n2/n1
- Refraction through glass slab: lateral displacement

**Total Internal Reflection**
- Critical angle: angle of incidence for which r = 90 degrees
- sin c = 1/n (for denser to rarer medium)
- Conditions for TIR: light from denser to rarer, i > c
- Applications: optical fiber, diamond brilliance, mirage, prism

**Lenses**
- Convex (converging) and concave (diverging) lenses
- Lens formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u
- Power of lens: P = 1/f (in diopters, D)
- Positive power for convex, negative for concave
- Magnification: m = v/u = h'/h
- Image formation by lenses

## Theme 10: Sound
### Content

**Wave Characteristics**
- Sound as longitudinal mechanical wave
- Frequency (f): number of vibrations per second, unit Hz
- Wavelength (lambda): distance between successive compressions
- Wave velocity: v = f x lambda
- Amplitude, loudness, and intensity
- Pitch and frequency relationship

**Speed of Sound**
- Speed in air: approximately 340 m/s at 20 degrees C
- Speed in different media: solid > liquid > gas
- Effect of temperature: v increases with T
- v = 331 + 0.6T (m/s) where T is in Celsius

**Echo and Reverberation**
- Echo: reflected sound heard as separate sound
- Conditions for echo: minimum time gap 0.1 s
- Minimum distance for echo in air: 17.2 m (at 20 degrees C)
- Echo calculations: distance = (v x t) / 2
- Reverberation: prolonged sound due to multiple reflections
- Reducing reverberation: acoustic materials

**Ultrasound and Infrasound**
- Audible range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
- Ultrasound: frequency > 20,000 Hz
- Applications: SONAR, medical imaging, cleaning, echolocation
- Infrasound: frequency < 20 Hz
- Natural sources and applications

**Human Ear**
- Basic structure: outer, middle, inner ear
- Function of eardrum, ossicles, cochlea
- Conversion of sound to nerve impulses

## Theme 11: Current Electricity
### Content

**Electric Current and Charge**
- Electric charge: Q, SI unit Coulomb (C)
- Electric current: I = Q/t
- SI unit: Ampere (A), 1 A = 1 C/s
- Conventional current vs electron flow
- Potential difference: V, unit Volt (V)

**Ohm's Law**
- Statement: V proportional to I (at constant temperature)
- V = IR
- Resistance: R = V/I, unit Ohm (Omega)
- Ohmic conductors: constant R (metals)
- Non-ohmic conductors: R varies (diode, filament lamp)

**Resistance and Resistivity**
- Factors affecting resistance: length (R proportional to L), area (R proportional to 1/A), material, temperature
- R = rho L/A
- Resistivity (rho): material property, unit Ohm-m
- Conductors, insulators, semiconductors

**Resistors in Series and Parallel**
- Series: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3...
- Same current through all, voltage divides
- Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3...
- Same voltage across all, current divides
- Combination circuits

**Electrical Power and Energy**
- Power: P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R
- SI unit: Watt (W)
- Energy: E = Pt = VIt
- Commercial unit: kWh (1 kWh = 3.6 x 10^6 J)
- Cost of electricity calculations
- Heating effect of current

## Theme 12: Magnetism
### Content

**Magnetic Field**
- Magnetic field: region where magnetic force acts
- Magnetic field lines: properties
- Field lines around bar magnet
- Earth's magnetic field: geographic and magnetic poles

**Magnetic Field Due to Current**
- Oersted's experiment: current produces magnetic field
- Right-hand thumb rule for straight conductor
- Field around straight conductor: concentric circles
- Field due to circular loop
- Field inside solenoid: uniform, like bar magnet

**Electromagnets**
- Electromagnet: temporary magnet using current
- Construction: soft iron core, insulated wire coil
- Factors affecting strength: current, number of turns, core material
- Applications: electric bell, magnetic crane, relay, circuit breaker

**Force on Current-Carrying Conductor**
- Force when conductor is in magnetic field
- Force is maximum when perpendicular to field
- Fleming's left-hand rule: direction of force
- F = BIL (for perpendicular orientation)

**Electric Motor (DC)**
- Principle: force on current-carrying conductor in magnetic field
- Construction: armature, commutator, brushes, field magnets
- Working: rotation of coil due to forces
- Role of commutator: reversing current direction
- Applications of motors

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 9 Physics, students should be able to:

- Use vernier caliper and screw gauge with zero error corrections
- Apply equations of motion to solve numerical problems
- Explain Newton's laws with examples and solve related problems
- Calculate gravitational force and understand variation of g
- Solve problems on fluid pressure, buoyancy, and floatation
- Apply work-energy theorem and conservation of energy
- Calculate MA, VR, and efficiency of simple machines
- Solve calorimetry problems using principle of heat exchange
- Apply mirror and lens formulas with sign conventions
- Explain total internal reflection and its applications
- Calculate echo distances and understand ultrasound applications
- Apply Ohm's law and solve circuit problems
- Calculate electrical power and energy consumption
- Explain electromagnetic effects and motor working

## Assessment

### Components
- Written Examination: 80 marks
- Internal Assessment / Practical Work: 20 marks
- Total: 100 marks

### Examination Pattern
- Section A: Short Answer (MCQs, fill in blanks, true/false) - 15 marks
- Section B: Short Answer (definitions, derivations, diagrams) - 25 marks
- Section C: Numericals and Applications - 25 marks
- Section D: Long Answer (detailed explanations, experiments) - 15 marks

### Practical Activities
- Measurement using vernier caliper and screw gauge
- Verification of equations of motion using ticker timer
- Determination of specific heat capacity
- Verification of Ohm's law
- Study of image formation by mirrors and lenses
- Finding focal length of convex lens
- Verification of Archimedes' principle
