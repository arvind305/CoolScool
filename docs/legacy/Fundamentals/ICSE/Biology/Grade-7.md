# ICSE Grade 7 Biology Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Biology syllabus for Grade 7 introduces students to fundamental concepts of life sciences including cell biology, tissues, nutrition, respiration, transportation, excretion, and reproduction. Students explore classification of living organisms and develop an understanding of ecosystems and environmental conservation. This curriculum lays the foundation for more advanced biology studies in higher classes.

---

## Theme 1: Cell - Structure and Function
### Content

**Discovery of Cell**
- Robert Hooke and the discovery of cells (1665)
- Anton van Leeuwenhoek and microscope observations
- Development of cell theory

**Cell Theory**
- All living things are made of cells
- Cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life
- All cells arise from pre-existing cells

**Cell Structure**

**Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)**
- Structure: thin, flexible, selectively permeable
- Functions: protection, control of substances entering and leaving

**Cell Wall (Plant Cells)**
- Structure: rigid, made of cellulose
- Functions: support, protection, shape

**Cytoplasm**
- Definition: jelly-like substance inside cell
- Contents: organelles suspended in cytosol
- Functions: site of chemical reactions

**Nucleus**
- Structure: nuclear membrane, nucleolus, chromatin
- Functions: controls cell activities, contains genetic material

**Cell Organelles**

**Mitochondria**
- Structure: double membrane, cristae
- Function: powerhouse of cell, energy production

**Endoplasmic Reticulum**
- Rough ER: with ribosomes, protein synthesis
- Smooth ER: lipid synthesis, detoxification

**Ribosomes**
- Structure: small, spherical
- Function: protein synthesis

**Golgi Apparatus (Golgi Body)**
- Structure: stacked membrane sacs
- Function: packaging and secretion of proteins

**Lysosomes**
- Structure: membrane-bound sacs
- Function: digestion of worn-out organelles, suicide bags

**Vacuoles**
- Plant cells: large central vacuole
- Animal cells: small, numerous vacuoles
- Function: storage of water, minerals, waste

**Plastids (Plant Cells)**
- Chloroplasts: contain chlorophyll, photosynthesis
- Chromoplasts: contain colored pigments
- Leucoplasts: storage of starch, oils, proteins

**Differences Between Plant and Animal Cells**
- Cell wall: present in plant, absent in animal
- Chloroplasts: present in plant, absent in animal
- Vacuoles: large central in plant, small in animal
- Centrioles: absent in plant, present in animal

**Unicellular and Multicellular Organisms**
- Unicellular: Amoeba, Paramecium, bacteria
- Multicellular: plants, animals, fungi
- Division of labor in multicellular organisms

## Theme 2: Plant Tissues and Organs
### Content

**Introduction to Tissues**
- Definition: group of similar cells performing same function
- Need for tissues in multicellular organisms

**Meristematic Tissues**
- Characteristics: thin-walled, actively dividing, no vacuoles
- Types based on location:
  - Apical meristem: tips of roots and shoots
  - Lateral meristem: sides of stems and roots
  - Intercalary meristem: base of leaves and internodes

**Permanent Tissues**

**Simple Permanent Tissues**

**Parenchyma**
- Structure: thin-walled, living cells
- Functions: storage, photosynthesis (chlorenchyma), buoyancy (aerenchyma)
- Location: throughout the plant

**Collenchyma**
- Structure: irregularly thickened corners, living
- Functions: support and flexibility
- Location: below epidermis in stems and petioles

**Sclerenchyma**
- Structure: thick lignified walls, dead cells
- Types: fibers and sclereids
- Functions: mechanical support
- Location: around vascular bundles

**Complex Permanent Tissues**

**Xylem (Introduction)**
- Components: vessels, tracheids, xylem parenchyma, xylem fibers
- Function: transport of water and minerals

**Phloem (Introduction)**
- Components: sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma
- Function: transport of food

**Root Structure and Function**
- Regions of root: root cap, zone of cell division, elongation, maturation
- Root hairs: absorption of water and minerals
- Types of roots: tap root, fibrous root, adventitious root
- Functions: anchorage, absorption, storage

**Stem Structure and Function**
- Functions: support, transport, storage
- Modifications: tubers, rhizomes, corms, bulbs

**Leaf Structure and Function**
- Parts: lamina, petiole, leaf base
- Venation: reticulate (dicots) and parallel (monocots)
- Functions: photosynthesis, transpiration, gaseous exchange
- Modifications: tendrils, spines, storage leaves

## Theme 3: Animal Tissues
### Content

**Types of Animal Tissues**
- Four main types: epithelial, connective, muscular, nervous

**Epithelial Tissue**
- Characteristics: closely packed cells, little intercellular matrix
- Functions: protection, secretion, absorption

**Types of Epithelial Tissue**
- Squamous: flat cells, lining of blood vessels, alveoli
- Cuboidal: cube-shaped, kidney tubules, salivary glands
- Columnar: tall cells, intestinal lining
- Ciliated: with cilia, respiratory tract, fallopian tubes
- Glandular: secretory function

**Connective Tissue**
- Characteristics: cells in matrix, supports and connects
- Types:
  - Blood: fluid connective tissue, RBC, WBC, platelets
  - Bone: hard matrix with calcium salts
  - Cartilage: flexible matrix, nose, ears
  - Adipose: fat storage, insulation
  - Areolar: loose tissue, packing material
  - Tendons: connect muscle to bone
  - Ligaments: connect bone to bone

**Muscular Tissue**
- Function: movement by contraction

**Types of Muscular Tissue**
- Striated (Skeletal) muscle:
  - Structure: cylindrical, multinucleate, striped
  - Control: voluntary
  - Location: attached to bones
- Unstriated (Smooth) muscle:
  - Structure: spindle-shaped, single nucleus, no stripes
  - Control: involuntary
  - Location: walls of digestive tract, blood vessels
- Cardiac muscle:
  - Structure: branched, single nucleus, faint stripes
  - Control: involuntary
  - Location: heart wall
  - Special feature: never fatigues

**Nervous Tissue**
- Function: coordination, impulse transmission
- Neuron structure: cell body, dendrites, axon
- Types: sensory, motor, association neurons

## Theme 4: Nutrition in Plants
### Content

**Modes of Nutrition**
- Autotrophic: organisms make their own food
- Heterotrophic: organisms depend on others for food

**Photosynthesis**
- Definition: process of making food using light energy
- Equation: Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen
- Site: chloroplasts in green leaves

**Requirements for Photosynthesis**
- Sunlight: source of energy
- Carbon dioxide: from air through stomata
- Water: from soil through roots
- Chlorophyll: green pigment in chloroplasts

**Products of Photosynthesis**
- Glucose: stored as starch
- Oxygen: released as by-product

**Stomata**
- Structure: guard cells and stomatal pore
- Functions: gas exchange, transpiration

**Test for Starch**
- Iodine test: blue-black color indicates starch

**Factors Affecting Photosynthesis**
- Light intensity: increases rate up to a point
- Carbon dioxide concentration: more CO2 increases rate
- Temperature: optimum temperature required
- Water availability: essential for the process

**Special Modes of Nutrition in Plants**

**Parasitic Plants**
- Definition: obtain nutrition from host plant
- Example: Cuscuta (dodder)
- Features: no chlorophyll, haustoria for absorption

**Insectivorous Plants**
- Definition: trap and digest insects
- Examples: Venus flytrap, sundew, pitcher plant
- Reason: grow in nitrogen-deficient soil

**Saprophytic Plants**
- Definition: obtain nutrition from dead organic matter
- Examples: fungi, some bacteria
- Role: decomposition

**Symbiotic Relationships**
- Lichens: algae and fungi living together
- Legume-Rhizobium: nitrogen fixation

**Nutrients Required by Plants**
- Macronutrients: nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium
- Micronutrients: iron, manganese, zinc, copper, boron
- Functions of major nutrients

## Theme 5: Nutrition in Animals
### Content

**Modes of Nutrition in Animals**
- Holozoic: ingestion of solid food (most animals)
- Saprophytic: feeding on dead matter
- Parasitic: living on or in host

**Steps in Holozoic Nutrition**
- Ingestion: taking in food
- Digestion: breaking down food
- Absorption: taking in nutrients
- Assimilation: using nutrients
- Egestion: removing undigested matter

**Human Digestive System**

**Alimentary Canal**

**Mouth (Buccal Cavity)**
- Teeth: types and functions
  - Incisors: cutting
  - Canines: tearing
  - Premolars and molars: grinding
- Tongue: taste, mixing, swallowing
- Saliva: contains salivary amylase, digests starch

**Oesophagus (Food Pipe)**
- Structure: muscular tube
- Peristalsis: wave-like movement pushes food

**Stomach**
- Structure: J-shaped muscular organ
- Gastric juice: HCl, pepsin, mucus
- Functions: churning, protein digestion

**Small Intestine**
- Three parts: duodenum, jejunum, ileum
- Bile from liver: emulsifies fats
- Pancreatic juice: contains enzymes for all nutrients
- Intestinal juice: completes digestion
- Villi: finger-like projections for absorption

**Large Intestine**
- Parts: caecum, colon, rectum
- Functions: absorption of water, formation of faeces

**Digestive Glands**

**Salivary Glands**
- Three pairs: parotid, submandibular, sublingual
- Produce saliva with amylase

**Liver**
- Largest gland in body
- Produces bile stored in gall bladder
- Functions: bile production, detoxification, storage

**Pancreas**
- Mixed gland: exocrine and endocrine
- Pancreatic juice: lipase, trypsin, amylase

**Nutrition in Other Animals**

**Amoeba**
- Holozoic nutrition
- Process: engulfs food by pseudopodia, food vacuole forms
- Digestion in food vacuole

**Ruminants (Cud-Chewing Animals)**
- Examples: cow, buffalo, goat
- Stomach with four chambers
- Regurgitation and re-chewing of cud
- Cellulose digestion by bacteria

## Theme 6: Respiration in Organisms
### Content

**Breathing and Respiration**
- Breathing: physical process of inhaling and exhaling
- Respiration: biochemical process of breaking down food for energy

**Types of Respiration**

**Aerobic Respiration**
- Definition: respiration using oxygen
- Equation: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
- Location: mitochondria
- Energy released: more (38 ATP)

**Anaerobic Respiration**
- Definition: respiration without oxygen
- In yeast: Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy
- In muscles: Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy
- Energy released: less (2 ATP)

**Human Respiratory System**

**Parts of Respiratory System**

**Nose (Nasal Cavity)**
- Functions: filtering, warming, moistening air
- Mucus and cilia trap dust and germs

**Pharynx**
- Common passage for air and food
- Epiglottis prevents food entering windpipe

**Larynx (Voice Box)**
- Contains vocal cords
- Functions: sound production

**Trachea (Windpipe)**
- Structure: C-shaped cartilage rings
- Functions: passage for air, kept open by cartilage

**Bronchi**
- Two branches from trachea
- Enter left and right lungs

**Lungs**
- Location: thoracic cavity, protected by ribs
- Structure: spongy, elastic organs
- Bronchioles: smaller branches of bronchi
- Alveoli: tiny air sacs for gas exchange

**Gas Exchange in Alveoli**
- Thin walls for easy diffusion
- Rich blood supply
- Large surface area
- Oxygen diffuses into blood
- Carbon dioxide diffuses out

**Mechanism of Breathing**

**Inhalation**
- Diaphragm contracts and flattens
- Ribs move up and out
- Chest cavity expands
- Air rushes in

**Exhalation**
- Diaphragm relaxes and domes up
- Ribs move down and in
- Chest cavity decreases
- Air pushed out

**Cellular Respiration**
- Takes place in mitochondria
- Glucose broken down step by step
- Energy released and stored in ATP

**Respiration in Other Organisms**

**Fish**
- Gills for respiration
- Water passes over gills
- Oxygen absorbed, carbon dioxide released

**Insects**
- Tracheal system
- Spiracles: openings for air
- Tracheae: tubes carrying air directly to cells

**Earthworm**
- Cutaneous respiration (through moist skin)
- Oxygen dissolves in moisture on skin

**Plants**
- Stomata in leaves
- Lenticels in bark
- Respiration occurs in all living cells

## Theme 7: Transportation in Plants and Animals
### Content

**Need for Transport**
- Movement of nutrients, oxygen, waste products
- Maintains life processes in all parts

**Transport in Plants**

**Transport of Water and Minerals**
- Xylem tissue: vessels and tracheids
- Root hair cells absorb water by osmosis
- Upward movement: root to leaves
- Forces: root pressure, transpiration pull, capillarity

**Transpiration**
- Definition: loss of water vapor through stomata
- Functions:
  - Creates transpiration pull
  - Cools the plant
  - Helps mineral transport

**Transport of Food**
- Phloem tissue: sieve tubes and companion cells
- Translocation: movement of food from leaves to other parts
- Direction: bidirectional (up and down)

**Transport in Animals**

**Human Circulatory System**
- Type: closed circulatory system
- Components: heart, blood, blood vessels

**Blood: Composition and Functions**

**Plasma**
- Fluid part of blood (55%)
- Functions: transport of nutrients, hormones, waste

**Red Blood Cells (RBCs)**
- Shape: biconcave disc, no nucleus
- Contain hemoglobin
- Function: oxygen transport

**White Blood Cells (WBCs)**
- Types: lymphocytes, monocytes, neutrophils
- Function: defense against infection

**Platelets**
- Cell fragments
- Function: blood clotting

**Human Heart**
- Location: thoracic cavity, slightly left
- Size: about the size of fist
- Structure: four chambers
  - Right atrium: receives deoxygenated blood
  - Right ventricle: pumps blood to lungs
  - Left atrium: receives oxygenated blood
  - Left ventricle: pumps blood to body
- Valves: prevent backflow of blood
- Heartbeat: contraction and relaxation

**Blood Vessels**

**Arteries**
- Structure: thick, muscular walls
- Function: carry blood away from heart
- Carry oxygenated blood (except pulmonary artery)

**Veins**
- Structure: thin walls, have valves
- Function: carry blood towards heart
- Carry deoxygenated blood (except pulmonary vein)

**Capillaries**
- Structure: very thin walls, one cell thick
- Function: exchange of materials between blood and tissues

**Blood Groups**
- ABO system: A, B, AB, O
- Importance in blood transfusion

## Theme 8: Excretion in Organisms
### Content

**Excretion**
- Definition: removal of metabolic waste from body
- Difference from egestion: excretion removes metabolic waste, egestion removes undigested food

**Metabolic Wastes**
- Carbon dioxide: from respiration
- Urea and uric acid: from protein breakdown
- Excess water and salts

**Human Excretory System**

**Organs of Excretion**
- Kidneys: main excretory organs
- Ureters: tubes from kidneys to bladder
- Urinary bladder: stores urine
- Urethra: passage for urine exit

**Kidneys**

**External Structure**
- Shape: bean-shaped
- Location: posterior abdominal wall
- One on each side of vertebral column
- Renal artery: brings blood to kidney
- Renal vein: takes blood from kidney

**Internal Structure**
- Cortex: outer region
- Medulla: inner region with pyramids
- Pelvis: funnel-shaped, collects urine
- Nephron: functional unit of kidney

**Nephron (Introduction)**
- Parts: Bowman's capsule, tubule
- Functions: filtration, reabsorption, secretion

**Urine Formation**
- Filtration: blood filtered in Bowman's capsule
- Reabsorption: useful substances taken back
- Secretion: additional waste added
- Composition of urine: water, urea, salts, uric acid

**Other Excretory Organs**

**Skin**
- Sweat glands produce sweat
- Sweat contains water, salts, urea

**Lungs**
- Remove carbon dioxide
- Remove water vapor

**Excretion in Other Organisms**

**Plants**
- No specialized excretory organs
- Methods:
  - Stomata: release CO2 and O2
  - Falling leaves: remove waste
  - Bark shedding
  - Storage in vacuoles

**Amoeba**
- Contractile vacuole removes excess water and waste

**Earthworm**
- Nephridia: excretory organs
- Excrete through skin and nephridia

## Theme 9: Reproduction in Plants
### Content

**Modes of Reproduction**
- Sexual reproduction: involves two parents, gametes, variation
- Asexual reproduction: single parent, no gametes, offspring identical

**Asexual Reproduction in Plants**

**Budding**
- Small outgrowth develops
- Examples: yeast, Hydra

**Fragmentation**
- Body breaks into fragments
- Each fragment grows into new organism
- Example: Spirogyra

**Spore Formation**
- Spores: tiny reproductive units
- Protected by tough covering
- Examples: ferns, fungi, mosses

**Vegetative Propagation**

**Natural Vegetative Propagation**
- Runners: strawberry
- Tubers: potato
- Bulbs: onion
- Rhizomes: ginger
- Corms: gladiolus

**Artificial Vegetative Propagation**
- Cutting: rose, sugarcane
- Layering: jasmine
- Grafting: mango, apple
- Advantages: faster, identical plants, seedless fruits

**Sexual Reproduction in Plants**

**Flower Structure**

**Parts of a Flower**
- Sepals: protect bud, usually green
- Petals: attract pollinators, colorful
- Stamens (male part): anther and filament
- Carpel/Pistil (female part): stigma, style, ovary

**Types of Flowers**
- Bisexual: both stamens and carpel
- Unisexual: either stamens or carpel
  - Male flowers: only stamens
  - Female flowers: only carpel

**Pollination**
- Definition: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma

**Types of Pollination**
- Self-pollination: same flower or same plant
- Cross-pollination: different plants of same species

**Agents of Pollination**
- Wind pollination: small, light pollen, feathery stigma
- Insect pollination: colorful, fragrant, nectar
- Water pollination: aquatic plants
- Animal pollination: birds, bats

**Fertilization**
- Definition: fusion of male and female gametes
- Pollen grain germinates on stigma
- Pollen tube grows down style
- Male gamete reaches ovule
- Fusion forms zygote

**Seed and Fruit Formation**
- Zygote develops into embryo
- Ovule becomes seed
- Ovary becomes fruit

**Seed Dispersal**
- By wind: light seeds, wings (maple, dandelion)
- By water: floating seeds (coconut)
- By animals: hooks, fleshy fruits
- By explosion: pods burst (peas)

## Theme 10: Classification of Plants and Animals
### Content

**Need for Classification**
- Millions of species on Earth
- Organization and study easier
- Understanding relationships

**Basis of Classification**
- Body organization: unicellular or multicellular
- Cell structure: prokaryotic or eukaryotic
- Mode of nutrition: autotrophic or heterotrophic
- Presence of cell wall, nucleus, organelles

**Hierarchical Classification**
- Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
- Example: Human classification

**Binomial Nomenclature**
- Two-name system for organisms
- Genus name + species name
- Examples: Homo sapiens, Mangifera indica

**Five Kingdom Classification (Whittaker)**

**Kingdom Monera**
- Prokaryotic, unicellular
- Examples: bacteria, blue-green algae

**Kingdom Protista**
- Eukaryotic, mostly unicellular
- Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena

**Kingdom Fungi**
- Eukaryotic, heterotrophic
- Cell wall of chitin
- Examples: mushrooms, yeast, molds

**Kingdom Plantae**
- Eukaryotic, autotrophic, multicellular
- Cell wall of cellulose
- Examples: all plants

**Kingdom Animalia**
- Eukaryotic, heterotrophic, multicellular
- No cell wall
- Examples: all animals

**Classification of Plants**

**Thallophyta**
- Simple body, no true roots, stems, leaves
- Examples: algae

**Bryophyta**
- No vascular tissue
- Examples: mosses, liverworts

**Pteridophyta**
- Have vascular tissue, no seeds
- Examples: ferns

**Gymnosperms**
- Naked seeds, not in fruit
- Examples: pine, cycas

**Angiosperms**
- Seeds enclosed in fruit
- Flowering plants

**Monocots vs Dicots**
- Cotyledons: one vs two
- Leaf venation: parallel vs reticulate
- Root system: fibrous vs tap
- Flower parts: in 3s vs 4s or 5s

**Classification of Animals**

**Invertebrates (Without Backbone)**
- Porifera: sponges
- Coelenterata: jellyfish, corals
- Platyhelminthes: flatworms
- Nematoda: roundworms
- Annelida: segmented worms
- Arthropoda: insects, spiders, crabs
- Mollusca: snails, octopus
- Echinodermata: starfish, sea urchin

**Vertebrates (With Backbone)**
- Pisces (Fish): gills, fins, cold-blooded
- Amphibia: moist skin, both land and water
- Reptilia: scales, lungs, cold-blooded
- Aves (Birds): feathers, wings, warm-blooded
- Mammalia: hair, mammary glands, warm-blooded

## Theme 11: Environment and Ecosystems
### Content

**Components of Environment**

**Biotic Components**
- Living things: plants, animals, microorganisms
- Producers: make their own food
- Consumers: depend on others for food
- Decomposers: break down dead matter

**Abiotic Components**
- Non-living things: air, water, soil, temperature, light
- Essential for survival of organisms

**Habitat and Adaptation**
- Habitat: place where organism lives
- Adaptation: features that help survival
- Examples: desert adaptations, aquatic adaptations

**Ecosystem**
- Definition: community of organisms with their physical environment
- Types: terrestrial (forest, desert, grassland) and aquatic (pond, ocean)

**Food Chains**
- Definition: sequence of who eats whom
- Starts with producer
- Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
- Each step is a trophic level

**Food Webs**
- Definition: interconnected food chains
- More realistic than single food chain
- Shows complex feeding relationships

**Energy Flow**
- Sun is ultimate source of energy
- Energy flows from producers to consumers
- Energy decreases at each trophic level
- Only 10% energy passes to next level

**Environmental Pollution**

**Air Pollution**
- Sources: vehicles, factories, burning
- Pollutants: smoke, dust, gases
- Effects: respiratory diseases, global warming

**Water Pollution**
- Sources: sewage, industrial waste, fertilizers
- Effects: water-borne diseases, aquatic life death

**Soil Pollution**
- Sources: pesticides, plastic, industrial waste
- Effects: loss of fertility, harmful to organisms

**Deforestation**
- Cutting down forests
- Causes: agriculture, urbanization, timber
- Effects: soil erosion, climate change, loss of biodiversity

**Conservation**
- Importance of biodiversity
- Endangered species protection
- National parks and wildlife sanctuaries
- Three Rs: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle
- Afforestation: planting trees

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 7 Biology, students should be able to:

- Describe cell structure and identify cell organelles
- Explain differences between plant and animal cells
- Identify types of plant and animal tissues
- Describe modes of nutrition in plants and animals
- Explain the process of photosynthesis
- Describe human digestive system and digestion process
- Explain respiration and human respiratory system
- Describe transport of materials in plants and animals
- Explain excretion in humans and other organisms
- Describe reproduction methods in plants
- Classify organisms into kingdoms
- Differentiate between vertebrates and invertebrates
- Explain ecosystem structure and food chains
- Identify causes and effects of pollution
- Appreciate importance of conservation

## Assessment

### Components
- Written Examination: 80 marks
- Internal Assessment / Practical Work: 20 marks
- Total: 100 marks

### Examination Pattern
- Section A: Very Short Answer (MCQs and one-word answers) - 20 marks
- Section B: Short Answer (definitions, diagrams, differences) - 30 marks
- Section C: Long Answer (detailed explanations, diagrams) - 30 marks

### Practical Activities
- Observation of plant and animal cells under microscope
- Test for starch in leaves
- Study of parts of a flower
- Study of human digestive system model
- Observation of breathing rate
- Study of different types of seeds
- Collection of information on endangered species
- Project on pollution and conservation
