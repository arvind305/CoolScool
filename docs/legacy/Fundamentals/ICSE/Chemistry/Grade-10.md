# ICSE Grade 10 Chemistry Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Chemistry syllabus for Grade 10 is designed for the board examination level, providing comprehensive coverage of fundamental chemical concepts, industrial processes, and practical applications. The curriculum emphasizes understanding of periodic trends, chemical bonding, industrial chemistry, and organic chemistry, preparing students for higher studies in science and engineering.

---

## Theme 1: Periodic Table and Periodicity
### Content

**Modern Periodic Law**
- Statement: Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers
- Comparison with Mendeleev's Periodic Law (based on atomic mass)
- Advantages of Modern Periodic Table

**Structure of Modern Periodic Table**
- Long form: 18 groups (vertical columns) and 7 periods (horizontal rows)
- Classification of elements:
  - s-block elements: Groups 1 and 2 (alkali and alkaline earth metals)
  - p-block elements: Groups 13 to 18
  - d-block elements: Groups 3 to 12 (transition elements)
  - f-block elements: Lanthanides and actinides (inner transition)

**Periods in the Periodic Table**
- Period number = number of electron shells
- First period: 2 elements (H, He)
- Second period: 8 elements (Li to Ne)
- Third period: 8 elements (Na to Ar)
- Fourth period onwards: includes d-block elements

**Groups in the Periodic Table**
- Group 1: Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
- Group 2: Alkaline earth metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra)
- Group 17: Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I, At)
- Group 18: Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn)
- Transition metals: Groups 3-12

**Periodic Trends**
- Atomic radius: decreases across a period (increased nuclear charge); increases down a group (more shells)
- Ionization energy: increases across a period; decreases down a group
- Electron affinity: generally increases across a period; decreases down a group
- Electronegativity: increases across a period; decreases down a group
- Metallic character: decreases across a period; increases down a group
- Non-metallic character: increases across a period; decreases down a group

**Position of Elements from Electronic Configuration**
- Period = number of shells occupied
- Group = number of valence electrons (for s and p block)
- Examples: Na (2,8,1) - Period 3, Group 1; Cl (2,8,7) - Period 3, Group 17

## Theme 2: Chemical Bonding
### Content

**The Octet Rule**
- Atoms tend to achieve 8 electrons in outermost shell (noble gas configuration)
- Duplet rule for helium (2 electrons)
- Methods: transfer of electrons (ionic) or sharing of electrons (covalent)

**Electrovalent (Ionic) Bonding**
- Formation by transfer of electrons from metal to non-metal
- Cation: positive ion formed by losing electrons
- Anion: negative ion formed by gaining electrons
- Electrovalency: number of electrons lost or gained
- Examples: NaCl, MgO, CaCl2, Al2O3
- Electron dot diagrams for ionic compound formation

**Covalent Bonding**
- Formation by sharing of electron pairs between non-metals
- Single bond: one shared pair (e.g., H2, Cl2, HCl)
- Double bond: two shared pairs (e.g., O2, CO2, C2H4)
- Triple bond: three shared pairs (e.g., N2, C2H2)
- Covalency: number of electron pairs shared
- Polar covalent bonds: unequal sharing (e.g., HCl, H2O)
- Non-polar covalent bonds: equal sharing (e.g., H2, Cl2)

**Coordinate (Dative) Bonding**
- Both shared electrons from one atom only
- Donor atom: provides both electrons
- Acceptor atom: accepts the electron pair
- Examples: NH4+ (from NH3 + H+), H3O+ (from H2O + H+)
- CO molecule, ozone (O3)
- Represented by arrow from donor to acceptor

**Lewis Dot Structures**
- Representing valence electrons as dots
- Drawing structures for molecules
- Identifying single, double, and triple bonds
- Identifying coordinate bonds

**Properties of Ionic and Covalent Compounds**

| Property | Ionic Compounds | Covalent Compounds |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Melting Point | High | Low |
| Boiling Point | High | Low |
| State at room temp | Usually solid | Solid, liquid, or gas |
| Solubility in water | Generally soluble | Often insoluble |
| Electrical conductivity | Conduct when molten/dissolved | Do not conduct |
| Crystal structure | Crystalline | May be amorphous |

## Theme 3: Acids, Bases, and Salts
### Content

**Definitions of Acids and Bases**
- Arrhenius Theory:
  - Acid: produces H+ ions in aqueous solution
  - Base: produces OH- ions in aqueous solution
- Bronsted-Lowry Theory:
  - Acid: proton (H+) donor
  - Base: proton (H+) acceptor
- Conjugate acid-base pairs

**Classification of Acids**
- Based on source: mineral acids, organic acids
- Based on basicity: monobasic (HCl), dibasic (H2SO4), tribasic (H3PO4)
- Strong acids: completely ionized (HCl, HNO3, H2SO4)
- Weak acids: partially ionized (CH3COOH, H2CO3)

**Classification of Bases**
- Alkalis: water-soluble bases
- Strong bases: completely ionized (NaOH, KOH)
- Weak bases: partially ionized (NH4OH, Mg(OH)2)

**The pH Scale**
- Measures hydrogen ion concentration
- Range: 0 to 14
- pH = -log[H+]
- pH < 7: acidic; pH = 7: neutral; pH > 7: basic
- Importance in everyday life and industry

**Indicators**
- Litmus: red in acid, blue in base
- Phenolphthalein: colorless in acid, pink in base
- Methyl orange: red in acid, yellow in base
- Universal indicator: shows range of colors for pH

**Neutralization Reactions**
- Acid + Base -> Salt + Water
- Exothermic reaction
- Applications: antacids, soil treatment, wastewater treatment

**Salts**
- Normal salts: complete neutralization (NaCl, Na2SO4)
- Acidic salts: incomplete neutralization of polybasic acid (NaHSO4)
- Basic salts: incomplete neutralization of polyacidic base (Mg(OH)Cl)
- Hydrolysis of salts: reaction with water
- Family of salts: chlorides, sulphates, nitrates, carbonates

**Action of Acids**
- On metals: metal + acid -> salt + hydrogen
- On metal oxides: metal oxide + acid -> salt + water
- On carbonates: carbonate + acid -> salt + water + CO2
- On bicarbonates: similar to carbonates

## Theme 4: Analytical Chemistry
### Content

**Qualitative Analysis: Introduction**
- Identification of cations and anions in salts
- Systematic approach to salt analysis
- Importance of careful observation

**Dry Tests for Cations**
- Color of salt: Cu2+ (blue), Fe3+ (yellow-brown), Fe2+ (green)
- Flame test:
  - Sodium: golden yellow
  - Potassium: lilac (violet)
  - Calcium: brick red
  - Barium: apple green
  - Copper: bluish green
- Charcoal cavity test
- Borax bead test

**Wet Tests for Cations**
- Action of NaOH solution:
  - Cu2+: blue precipitate, insoluble in excess
  - Fe2+: green precipitate, turns brown
  - Fe3+: reddish-brown precipitate
  - Zn2+: white precipitate, soluble in excess
  - Al3+: white precipitate, soluble in excess
  - Pb2+: white precipitate, soluble in excess
- Action of NH4OH solution:
  - Cu2+: blue precipitate, soluble in excess (deep blue)
  - Zn2+: white precipitate, soluble in excess
  - Fe3+: reddish-brown precipitate, insoluble in excess

**Anion Analysis**
- Carbonate (CO3^2-): effervescence with dilute acid, gas turns lime water milky
- Sulphate (SO4^2-): white precipitate with BaCl2, insoluble in HNO3
- Chloride (Cl-): white curdy precipitate with AgNO3, soluble in NH4OH
- Nitrate (NO3-): brown ring test with FeSO4 and conc. H2SO4

**Confirmatory Tests**
- Specific tests to confirm presence of ions
- Recording observations systematically
- Writing ionic equations for reactions

## Theme 5: Mole Concept and Stoichiometry
### Content

**The Mole Concept**
- Mole: SI unit for amount of substance
- One mole contains 6.022 x 10^23 particles (Avogadro's number)
- Molar mass: mass of one mole in grams

**Atomic Mass and Molecular Mass**
- Relative atomic mass (Ar): compared to 1/12 of C-12
- Relative molecular mass (Mr): sum of atomic masses in molecule
- Gram atomic mass and gram molecular mass

**Molar Volume**
- At STP (0 C, 1 atm): one mole of any gas occupies 22.4 L
- Relationship: n = V/22.4 (at STP)

**Important Relationships**
- Number of moles = mass/molar mass
- Number of moles = number of particles/Avogadro's number
- Number of moles = volume at STP/22.4

**Stoichiometry**
- Calculation based on balanced equations
- Mole ratios from coefficients
- Mass-mass calculations
- Mass-volume calculations (gases at STP)
- Volume-volume calculations (gases)

**Percentage Composition**
- Percentage of element = (mass of element in compound/molecular mass) x 100
- Calculation from formula

**Empirical and Molecular Formula**
- Empirical formula: simplest whole number ratio
- Molecular formula: actual number of atoms
- Molecular formula = n x Empirical formula
- Calculation from percentage composition

## Theme 6: Electrolysis
### Content

**Electrolytes and Non-electrolytes**
- Electrolyte: conducts electricity in molten or aqueous state
- Strong electrolytes: completely ionized (NaCl, HCl, NaOH)
- Weak electrolytes: partially ionized (CH3COOH, NH4OH)
- Non-electrolyte: does not conduct (sugar, urea, alcohol)

**The Electrolytic Cell**
- Components: electrolyte, electrodes (cathode and anode), power source
- Cathode: negative electrode, site of reduction
- Anode: positive electrode, site of oxidation
- Migration of ions during electrolysis

**Electrolysis of Molten Compounds**
- Lead bromide (PbBr2):
  - Cathode: Pb2+ + 2e- -> Pb (lead deposits)
  - Anode: 2Br- -> Br2 + 2e- (bromine gas)
- Sodium chloride (NaCl):
  - Cathode: Na+ + e- -> Na
  - Anode: 2Cl- -> Cl2 + 2e-

**Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions**
- Acidified water:
  - Cathode: 4H+ + 4e- -> 2H2
  - Anode: 4OH- -> 2H2O + O2 + 4e-
  - Volume ratio H2:O2 = 2:1
- Copper sulphate with copper electrodes:
  - Cathode: Cu2+ + 2e- -> Cu (copper deposits)
  - Anode: Cu -> Cu2+ + 2e- (copper dissolves)
- Copper sulphate with platinum electrodes:
  - Oxygen liberated at anode

**Electrolysis of Brine**
- Products: NaOH, H2, Cl2
- Industrial importance (chlor-alkali process)

**Applications of Electrolysis**
- Electroplating: decorative and protective coating
- Electrorefining: purification of metals
- Electrotyping: making printing plates
- Extraction of metals: Na, Al, Mg

## Theme 7: Metallurgy
### Content

**Minerals and Ores**
- Mineral: naturally occurring compound of metal
- Ore: mineral from which metal can be extracted profitably
- Gangue: impurities in ore
- Common ores:
  - Iron: Haematite (Fe2O3), Magnetite (Fe3O4)
  - Aluminium: Bauxite (Al2O3.2H2O)
  - Zinc: Calamine (ZnCO3), Zinc blende (ZnS)
  - Copper: Copper pyrites (CuFeS2)

**Concentration of Ores**
- Gravity separation: washing with water
- Magnetic separation: for magnetic ores
- Froth flotation: for sulphide ores
- Leaching: for oxide ores (bauxite)

**Extraction Based on Reactivity**
- Highly reactive metals (Na, K, Ca, Mg, Al):
  - Extracted by electrolysis of molten compounds
- Medium reactive metals (Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu):
  - Reduction with carbon or carbon monoxide
- Less reactive metals (Hg, Ag, Au):
  - Roasting or heating in air

**Extraction of Iron: Blast Furnace**
- Raw materials: haematite, coke, limestone
- Reactions:
  - C + O2 -> CO2 (combustion zone)
  - CO2 + C -> 2CO (reduction zone)
  - Fe2O3 + 3CO -> 2Fe + 3CO2
  - CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2 (limestone decomposition)
  - CaO + SiO2 -> CaSite (slag formation)
- Products: pig iron, slag

**Extraction of Aluminium: Hall-Heroult Process**
- Purification of bauxite: Bayer's process
- Electrolysis of Al2O3 dissolved in molten cryolite
- Cathode: Al3+ + 3e- -> Al
- Anode: O2- -> O2 + 2e- (reacts with carbon)

**Alloys**
- Definition: homogeneous mixture of metals
- Purpose: improve properties
- Important alloys:
  - Brass: Cu + Zn (golden, decorative items)
  - Bronze: Cu + Sn (statues, medals)
  - Steel: Fe + C (construction)
  - Stainless steel: Fe + C + Cr + Ni (utensils)
  - Duralumin: Al + Cu + Mg + Mn (aircraft)
  - Solder: Pb + Sn (joining metals)

**Corrosion**
- Rusting of iron: 4Fe + 3O2 + 2xH2O -> 2Fe2O3.xH2O
- Conditions: presence of air (oxygen) and moisture
- Prevention:
  - Painting, oiling, greasing
  - Galvanizing (zinc coating)
  - Electroplating
  - Alloying (stainless steel)
  - Sacrificial protection

## Theme 8: Study of Compounds
### Part A: Ammonia (NH3)

**Laboratory Preparation**
- Heating ammonium salt with alkali:
  - 2NH4Cl + Ca(OH)2 -> CaCl2 + 2H2O + 2NH3
  - NH4Cl + NaOH -> NaCl + H2O + NH3
- Drying agent: quicklime (CaO) - not conc. H2SO4
- Collection: downward displacement of air

**Industrial Manufacture: Haber Process**
- Reaction: N2 + 3H2 <-> 2NH3
- Conditions:
  - Temperature: 450-500 C
  - Pressure: 200-300 atm
  - Catalyst: finely divided iron with promoters
- Reversible reaction: optimum conditions needed

**Physical Properties**
- Colorless gas with pungent choking smell
- Lighter than air (density 0.59 g/L at STP)
- Extremely soluble in water (700 volumes in 1 volume water)
- Liquefies easily under pressure

**Chemical Properties**
- Basic nature: turns red litmus blue, forms NH4OH in water
- Reaction with acids: NH3 + HCl -> NH4Cl (white fumes)
- Combustion: 4NH3 + 3O2 -> 2N2 + 6H2O
- Reaction with chlorine: 8NH3 + 3Cl2 -> 6NH4Cl + N2
- Reducing property: reduces CuO to Cu
  - 3CuO + 2NH3 -> 3Cu + 3H2O + N2

**Tests for Ammonia**
- Turns moist red litmus blue
- Forms white fumes with HCl vapors
- Characteristic pungent smell

**Uses**
- Manufacture of fertilizers (ammonium sulphate, ammonium nitrate)
- Manufacture of nitric acid (Ostwald process)
- Refrigerant (in ice plants)
- Manufacture of explosives, nylon, plastics

### Part B: Nitric Acid (HNO3)

**Laboratory Preparation**
- Heating nitrate salt with concentrated H2SO4:
  - NaNO3 + H2SO4 -> NaHSO4 + HNO3
- Temperature: below 200 C (prevents decomposition)
- Collection by distillation

**Industrial Manufacture: Ostwald Process**
- Step 1: Oxidation of ammonia
  - 4NH3 + 5O2 -> 4NO + 6H2O (Pt-Rh catalyst, 800 C)
- Step 2: Oxidation of nitric oxide
  - 2NO + O2 -> 2NO2
- Step 3: Absorption in water
  - 4NO2 + O2 + 2H2O -> 4HNO3

**Physical Properties**
- Colorless liquid when pure
- Turns yellow/brown on standing (decomposition to NO2)
- Boiling point: 86 C
- Highly corrosive, causes yellow stains on skin

**Chemical Properties**
- Acidic properties: with bases, carbonates, metals
- Oxidizing nature (concentrated):
  - Cu + 4HNO3(conc.) -> Cu(NO3)2 + 2NO2 + 2H2O
  - C + 4HNO3 -> CO2 + 4NO2 + 2H2O
- Dilute acid with metals:
  - 3Cu + 8HNO3(dil.) -> 3Cu(NO3)2 + 2NO + 4H2O
- Passivity: Al, Fe become passive with conc. HNO3
- Aqua regia: 3HCl + HNO3 (dissolves gold and platinum)

**Uses**
- Manufacture of fertilizers (ammonium nitrate)
- Manufacture of explosives (TNT, dynamite)
- In dyes, drugs, and perfumes
- Etching designs on metals

### Part C: Sulphuric Acid (H2SO4)

**Industrial Manufacture: Contact Process**
- Step 1: Production of SO2
  - S + O2 -> SO2 (burning sulphur)
  - Or roasting of sulphide ores
- Step 2: Oxidation of SO2
  - 2SO2 + O2 -> 2SO3 (V2O5 catalyst, 450 C)
- Step 3: Formation of oleum
  - SO3 + H2SO4 -> H2S2O7 (oleum)
- Step 4: Dilution
  - H2S2O7 + H2O -> 2H2SO4

**Physical Properties**
- Colorless, viscous, oily liquid
- High boiling point: 338 C
- High density: 1.84 g/cm3
- Highly soluble in water (exothermic)
- Dilution: always add acid to water, never water to acid

**Chemical Properties**
- Acidic properties: reaction with bases, carbonates
- Dibasic acid: forms normal and acidic salts
- Dehydrating property:
  - Removes water from compounds
  - Sugar chars to carbon: C12H22O11 -> 12C + 11H2O
  - CuSO4.5H2O -> CuSO4 (white) + 5H2O
- Oxidizing property (concentrated, hot):
  - Cu + 2H2SO4(conc.) -> CuSO4 + SO2 + 2H2O
  - C + 2H2SO4 -> CO2 + 2SO2 + 2H2O
  - S + 2H2SO4 -> 3SO2 + 2H2O

**Tests for Sulphuric Acid/Sulphate**
- BaCl2 solution: white precipitate of BaSO4, insoluble in HNO3

**Uses**
- King of chemicals (most widely used industrial chemical)
- Manufacture of fertilizers (superphosphate)
- Manufacture of detergents, dyes, drugs
- Petroleum refining
- Storage batteries (lead-acid)
- Manufacture of other acids

## Theme 9: Organic Chemistry
### Part A: Introduction to Organic Chemistry

**Definition and Characteristics**
- Organic chemistry: study of carbon compounds
- Vital force theory: organic compounds only from living organisms
- Wohler's synthesis of urea (1828): disproved vital force theory
- Characteristics: covalent bonding, low melting/boiling points, flammable

**Catenation**
- Ability of carbon to form bonds with other carbon atoms
- Forms straight chains, branched chains, and rings
- Reason for large number of organic compounds

**Tetravalency of Carbon**
- Carbon has 4 valence electrons
- Forms 4 covalent bonds
- Can form single, double, or triple bonds

**Homologous Series**
- Family of organic compounds with same functional group
- Differ by -CH2- unit
- Same general formula
- Similar chemical properties
- Gradation in physical properties

**Functional Groups**
- Alcohol: -OH (hydroxyl)
- Aldehyde: -CHO
- Ketone: >C=O
- Carboxylic acid: -COOH
- Halogen: -X (Cl, Br, I)
- Amine: -NH2

**IUPAC Nomenclature**
- Prefix: substituents and their positions
- Root: number of carbon atoms (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-)
- Suffix: functional group (-ane, -ene, -yne, -ol, -al, -one, -oic acid)

**Isomerism**
- Same molecular formula, different structural arrangement
- Chain isomerism: different carbon skeletons
- Position isomerism: different positions of functional group
- Functional group isomerism: different functional groups

### Part B: Hydrocarbons

**Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)**
- General formula: CnH2n+2
- Single bonds only (saturated)
- Names: methane, ethane, propane, butane
- Properties: combustion, substitution (halogenation)
- Chlorination of methane: CH4 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + HCl (in light)

**Preparation of Methane**
- Laboratory: heating sodium acetate with soda lime
  - CH3COONa + NaOH -> CH4 + Na2CO3
- Collection: over water or by downward displacement of air

**Alkenes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)**
- General formula: CnH2n
- Contain C=C double bond
- Names: ethene, propene, butene
- Properties: addition reactions
- Hydrogenation: C2H4 + H2 -> C2H6 (Ni catalyst)
- Bromination: C2H4 + Br2 -> C2H4Br2 (decolorizes bromine water)

**Preparation of Ethene**
- Dehydration of ethanol:
  - C2H5OH -> C2H4 + H2O (conc. H2SO4, 170 C)
  - Or Al2O3 at 350 C

**Alkynes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)**
- General formula: CnH2n-2
- Contain Câ‰¡C triple bond
- Names: ethyne (acetylene), propyne

**Preparation of Ethyne**
- Calcium carbide with water:
  - CaC2 + 2H2O -> C2H2 + Ca(OH)2
- Collection: over water

**Uses of Hydrocarbons**
- Fuels: LPG, petrol, diesel, natural gas
- Plastics: polyethene, polypropene
- Solvents: benzene, toluene
- Starting materials for chemical industry

### Part C: Alcohols and Carboxylic Acids

**Alcohols**
- Functional group: -OH (hydroxyl)
- Classification:
  - Primary: -CH2OH (one C attached)
  - Secondary: >CHOH (two C attached)
  - Tertiary: -C(OH)- (three C attached)

**Ethanol (C2H5OH)**
- Structure: CH3-CH2-OH
- Preparation: fermentation of sugars
  - C6H12O6 -> 2C2H5OH + 2CO2 (yeast enzyme)
- Industrial: hydration of ethene
  - C2H4 + H2O -> C2H5OH (acid catalyst, high pressure)
- Properties:
  - Colorless liquid, pleasant smell
  - Miscible with water (hydrogen bonding)
  - Combustion: C2H5OH + 3O2 -> 2CO2 + 3H2O
  - Reaction with Na: 2C2H5OH + 2Na -> 2C2H5ONa + H2
  - Dehydration: forms ethene at 170 C
  - Oxidation: forms ethanoic acid

**Carboxylic Acids**
- Functional group: -COOH
- Ethanoic acid (CH3COOH): vinegar
- Properties:
  - Weak acid: partially ionized
  - Sour taste, pungent smell
  - Reaction with bases: forms salts
  - Reaction with carbonates: effervescence (CO2)

**Esterification**
- Alcohol + Carboxylic acid -> Ester + Water
- CH3COOH + C2H5OH -> CH3COOC2H5 + H2O
- Conc. H2SO4 as catalyst
- Esters: fruity smell, used in perfumes, flavors

**Saponification**
- Ester + Alkali -> Alcohol + Salt of acid
- Soap making: fat + NaOH -> glycerol + soap
- Hydrolysis of ester with alkali

## Theme 10: Practical Chemistry
### Content

**Laboratory Safety**
- Proper handling of acids and bases
- Heating techniques (direct and water bath)
- Gas collection safety precautions
- First aid for chemical burns and spills

**Common Laboratory Apparatus**
- Burette, pipette, conical flask, beaker
- Bunsen burner, tripod stand, wire gauze
- Test tubes, boiling tubes, round-bottom flask
- Delivery tubes, gas jars, troughs

**Preparation of Gases**

| Gas | Reagents | Collection Method | Drying Agent |
|-----|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| H2 | Zn + dil. HCl | Over water | Anhydrous CaCl2 |
| O2 | H2O2 + MnO2 | Over water | Conc. H2SO4 |
| CO2 | CaCO3 + dil. HCl | Upward displacement | Anhydrous CaCl2 |
| Cl2 | HCl + MnO2 | Upward displacement | Conc. H2SO4 |
| HCl | NaCl + conc. H2SO4 | Downward displacement | Not dried usually |
| NH3 | NH4Cl + NaOH | Downward displacement | Quicklime (CaO) |
| SO2 | Na2SO3 + dil. HCl | Upward displacement | Conc. H2SO4 |

**Tests for Common Gases**
- H2: Burns with pop sound
- O2: Relights glowing splint
- CO2: Turns lime water milky
- Cl2: Bleaches moist litmus, pungent smell
- HCl: White fumes with ammonia
- NH3: Turns moist red litmus blue
- SO2: Pungent smell, turns K2Cr2O7 green

**Acid-Base Titrations**
- Principle: neutralization reaction
- Indicator selection based on acid-base strength
- End point identification
- Calculations: M1V1 = M2V2

**Salt Analysis Procedure**
- Physical examination: color, smell, texture
- Dry tests: heating, flame test
- Wet tests: with NaOH, NH4OH
- Anion tests: with appropriate reagents
- Confirmatory tests
- Recording observations in tabular form

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 10 Chemistry, students should be able to:

- Explain the Modern Periodic Law and trends in the periodic table
- Distinguish between ionic, covalent, and coordinate bonding
- Write electron dot structures for molecules
- Explain properties of acids, bases, and salts
- Perform qualitative analysis of salts
- Apply mole concept to stoichiometric calculations
- Explain the process of electrolysis and its applications
- Describe extraction of metals from ores
- Explain industrial manufacture of ammonia, nitric acid, and sulphuric acid
- Identify functional groups in organic compounds
- Write IUPAC names for simple organic compounds
- Explain properties and reactions of hydrocarbons, alcohols, and acids
- Perform laboratory techniques safely and accurately
