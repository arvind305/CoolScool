# ICSE Grade 7 Chemistry Syllabus
## 2025-2026 Academic Year

**Indian Certificate of Secondary Education (ICSE) - Official Curriculum**

The ICSE Chemistry syllabus for Grade 7 introduces students to fundamental concepts of chemistry, including the nature of matter, classification of substances, and basic chemical reactions. The curriculum emphasizes observation-based learning and understanding of chemistry in everyday life, building a foundation for more advanced chemistry studies in subsequent grades.

---

## Theme 1: Matter - Classification and Properties
### Content

**What is Matter?**
- Definition: anything that has mass and occupies space
- Examples of matter in daily life
- Characteristics of matter

**States of Matter**
- The Solid State: definite shape and definite volume
- The Liquid State: no definite shape, definite volume
- The Gaseous State: no definite shape, no definite volume
- Comparison of three states

**Properties of Matter**
- Physical properties: color, odor, hardness, density
- Chemical properties: reactivity, combustibility
- Measuring properties: melting point, boiling point

**Interconversion of States**
- Melting: solid to liquid
- Freezing: liquid to solid
- Evaporation: liquid to gas
- Condensation: gas to liquid
- Sublimation: solid to gas directly
- Examples of state changes in daily life

---

## Theme 2: Atoms and Molecules
### Content

**Introduction to Atoms**
- Atom: smallest particle of an element
- Atoms are too small to see
- All matter is made of atoms
- Atoms of same element are identical

**Introduction to Molecules**
- Molecule: smallest particle of a substance with independent existence
- Molecules are made of atoms
- Atoms combine to form molecules

**Types of Molecules**
- Monoatomic molecules: He, Ne, Ar
- Diatomic molecules: H2, O2, N2, Cl2
- Polyatomic molecules: P4, S8, O3

**Molecules of Compounds**
- Molecules made of different atoms
- Examples: H2O (water), CO2 (carbon dioxide), NH3 (ammonia)
- Difference between atoms and molecules

**Atomicity**
- Number of atoms in a molecule
- Atomicity of common elements

---

## Theme 3: Elements, Compounds and Mixtures
### Content

**Pure Substances**
- Fixed composition and properties
- Elements and compounds are pure substances

**Elements**
- Simplest form of matter
- Cannot be broken down further
- Made of only one type of atom

**Classification of Elements**
- Metals: iron, copper, gold, silver, aluminium
- Non-metals: carbon, sulfur, oxygen, nitrogen

**Properties of Metals**
- Lustrous (shiny)
- Malleable (can be beaten into sheets)
- Ductile (can be drawn into wires)
- Good conductors of heat and electricity

**Properties of Non-metals**
- Generally dull
- Brittle
- Poor conductors of heat and electricity

**Compounds**
- Formed by chemical combination of elements
- Fixed composition
- Properties differ from constituent elements
- Examples: water, salt, sugar

**Mixtures**
- Physical combination of substances
- Variable composition
- Homogeneous mixtures: uniform throughout
- Heterogeneous mixtures: not uniform

**Separation of Mixtures**
- Filtration: separating insoluble solid from liquid
- Evaporation: separating dissolved solid from liquid
- Distillation: separating liquids with different boiling points
- Handpicking and sieving

## Theme 4: Physical and Chemical Changes
### Content

**Physical Changes**
- No new substance is formed
- Change in shape, size, or state
- Usually reversible
- Examples: melting ice, breaking glass, dissolving salt

**Chemical Changes**
- New substance is formed
- Change in composition
- Usually irreversible
- Examples: rusting, burning, cooking

**Indicators of Chemical Change**
- Change in color
- Evolution of gas
- Change in temperature
- Formation of precipitate
- Change in smell

**Reversible and Irreversible Changes**
- Reversible: can be undone
- Irreversible: cannot be undone
- Examples of each type

**Differences Between Physical and Chemical Changes**
- Comparison of characteristics
- Identifying changes in daily life

## Theme 5: Acids, Bases and Indicators
### Content

**Acids**
- Sour taste
- Turn blue litmus red
- Examples: vinegar, lemon juice, citric acid
- Strong acids: hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid, nitric acid

**Bases**
- Bitter taste
- Soapy or slippery feel
- Turn red litmus blue
- Examples: soap solution, baking soda
- Strong bases: sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide

**Alkalis**
- Bases that dissolve in water
- Examples: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)2

**Indicators**
- Substances that show different colors in acids and bases
- Litmus: natural indicator from lichens
- Blue litmus turns red in acid
- Red litmus turns blue in base

**Natural Indicators**
- Turmeric: yellow in neutral/acid, red in base
- Red cabbage juice: changes color in acid and base
- China rose (hibiscus) solution

**Synthetic Indicators**
- Phenolphthalein: colorless in acid, pink in base
- Methyl orange: red in acid, yellow in base

**Neutralization**
- Acid + Base gives Salt + Water
- Properties of resulting solution
- Applications: antacids, soil treatment

## Theme 6: Metals and Non-Metals
### Content

**Physical Properties of Metals**
- Lustre: shiny appearance
- Malleability: can be beaten into thin sheets
- Ductility: can be drawn into wires
- Conductivity: good conductors of heat and electricity
- Sonority: produce ringing sound when struck
- High melting and boiling points (generally)

**Exceptions in Metals**
- Mercury: liquid at room temperature
- Sodium and potassium: soft metals
- Sodium: low density, floats on water

**Physical Properties of Non-Metals**
- Generally dull appearance
- Brittle: break when hammered
- Poor conductors of heat and electricity
- Low melting and boiling points (generally)

**Exceptions in Non-Metals**
- Diamond: hardest known substance
- Graphite: good conductor of electricity
- Iodine: has lustre

**Chemical Properties of Metals**
- Reaction with oxygen: forms metal oxide
- Example: 4Na + O2 gives 2Na2O
- Reaction with water: some metals react
- Example: 2Na + 2H2O gives 2NaOH + H2
- Reaction with acids: gives salt and hydrogen
- Example: Zn + 2HCl gives ZnCl2 + H2

**Uses of Metals and Non-Metals**
- Based on their properties
- Iron: construction, machines
- Copper: electrical wires
- Aluminium: utensils, foils
- Gold, Silver: jewelry
- Carbon: fuels, pencils
- Sulfur: matches, medicines

## Theme 7: Air - Composition and Pollution
### Content

**Composition of Air**
- Air is a mixture of gases
- Nitrogen: approximately 78%
- Oxygen: approximately 21%
- Other gases: approximately 1%
- Other gases include: carbon dioxide, water vapor, noble gases

**Properties of Nitrogen**
- Colorless, odorless gas
- Does not support combustion
- Does not support respiration
- Used in food packaging

**Properties of Oxygen**
- Colorless, odorless gas
- Supports combustion
- Essential for respiration
- Test: glowing splint bursts into flame

**Properties of Carbon Dioxide**
- Colorless gas with slight odor
- Does not support combustion
- Test: turns lime water milky
- Used in fire extinguishers

**Air Pollution**
- Addition of harmful substances to air
- Pollutants: gases and particles

**Sources of Air Pollution**
- Vehicles: carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides
- Industries: sulfur dioxide, smoke
- Burning of fuels: carbon dioxide, particulates
- Agricultural activities: methane, ammonia

**Effects of Air Pollution**
- Respiratory diseases
- Acid rain
- Global warming
- Damage to plants and animals

**Prevention of Air Pollution**
- Use of clean fuels
- Catalytic converters in vehicles
- Industrial emission controls
- Planting trees
- Reducing, reusing, recycling

## Theme 8: Water - Properties and Solutions
### Content

**Physical Properties of Water**
- Colorless, odorless, tasteless liquid
- Boiling point: 100 degrees Celsius
- Freezing point: 0 degrees Celsius
- Exists in three states

**Water as a Solvent**
- Universal solvent: dissolves many substances
- Not all substances dissolve in water
- Factors affecting solubility

**Solutions**
- Homogeneous mixture of solute and solvent
- Solute: substance that dissolves
- Solvent: substance in which solute dissolves
- Examples: sugar in water, salt in water

**Types of Solutions**
- Unsaturated solution: can dissolve more solute
- Saturated solution: cannot dissolve more solute
- Supersaturated solution: contains more solute than possible

**Suspensions**
- Particles do not dissolve
- Particles settle down on standing
- Examples: muddy water, chalk in water

**Water Cycle**
- Evaporation: water to water vapor
- Condensation: water vapor to water
- Precipitation: rain, snow, sleet
- Collection: oceans, rivers, groundwater

**Importance of Water**
- Essential for life
- Used in agriculture, industry
- Habitat for aquatic organisms

**Water Conservation**
- Importance of saving water
- Methods of water conservation
- Rainwater harvesting

## Theme 9: Symbols and Formulae
### Content

**Chemical Symbols**
- Short representation of elements
- One or two letters
- First letter always capital
- Second letter always small

**Symbols from English Names**
- Hydrogen: H
- Oxygen: O
- Carbon: C
- Nitrogen: N
- Sulfur: S
- Phosphorus: P
- Chlorine: Cl
- Calcium: Ca
- Magnesium: Mg
- Aluminium: Al
- Zinc: Zn

**Symbols from Latin Names**
- Iron: Fe (Ferrum)
- Copper: Cu (Cuprum)
- Silver: Ag (Argentum)
- Gold: Au (Aurum)
- Sodium: Na (Natrium)
- Potassium: K (Kalium)
- Lead: Pb (Plumbum)
- Mercury: Hg (Hydrargyrum)

**Introduction to Valency**
- Combining capacity of an element
- Valency of hydrogen: 1
- Valency of oxygen: 2
- Valency of nitrogen: 3
- Valency of carbon: 4

**Writing Simple Formulae**
- Cross-multiply the valencies
- Simplify if possible
- Examples: H2O, CO2, NaCl

**Common Chemical Formulae**
- Water: H2O
- Carbon dioxide: CO2
- Ammonia: NH3
- Sodium chloride: NaCl
- Calcium carbonate: CaCO3
- Hydrochloric acid: HCl
- Sulfuric acid: H2SO4

## Theme 10: Simple Chemical Reactions
### Content

**What is a Chemical Reaction?**
- Process where new substances are formed
- Reactants: starting materials
- Products: substances formed
- Evidence of chemical reaction

**Word Equations**
- Describing reactions in words
- Reactants on left, products on right
- Arrow shows direction of reaction
- Example: Iron + Oxygen gives Iron oxide

**Combination Reactions**
- Two or more substances combine
- Form a single product
- Example: Magnesium + Oxygen gives Magnesium oxide

**Decomposition Reactions**
- Single substance breaks down
- Forms two or more products
- Example: Water gives Hydrogen + Oxygen (electrolysis)
- Example: Calcium carbonate gives Calcium oxide + Carbon dioxide (heating)

**Energy Changes in Reactions**
- Exothermic reactions: release heat
- Examples: burning, rusting
- Endothermic reactions: absorb heat
- Examples: photosynthesis, dissolving ammonium chloride

**Everyday Chemical Reactions**
- Cooking food
- Digestion
- Rusting of iron
- Burning of fuels
- Respiration

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of Grade 7 Chemistry, students should be able to:

- Define matter and describe its states
- Differentiate between atoms and molecules
- Classify substances as elements, compounds, or mixtures
- Distinguish between physical and chemical changes
- Identify acids and bases using indicators
- Compare properties of metals and non-metals
- Describe composition of air and effects of pollution
- Explain properties of water and types of solutions
- Write chemical symbols of common elements
- Write simple chemical formulae
- Describe simple chemical reactions using word equations

## Assessment

### Components
- Written Examination: 80 marks
- Internal Assessment / Practical Work: 20 marks
- Total: 100 marks

### Examination Pattern
- Section A: Very Short Answer (MCQs and fill in the blanks) - 20 marks
- Section B: Short Answer (definitions, examples) - 30 marks
- Section C: Long Answer (explanations, diagrams) - 30 marks

### Practical Activities
- Testing substances with indicators
- Separation of mixtures
- Observing physical and chemical changes
- Testing properties of metals and non-metals
- Preparing solutions of different concentrations
- Observing combustion and other reactions
